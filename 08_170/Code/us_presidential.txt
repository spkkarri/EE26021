George W. Bush was elected as the 43rd president of the United States in the year 2000, defeating Democratic candidate Al Gore in one of the most contested elections in American history. The election came down to the state of Florida, where the margin of victory was so slim that a manual recount was ordered. The Supreme Court ultimately halted the recount in a five to four decision, effectively handing the presidency to Bush. Al Gore, the sitting vice president under Bill Clinton, won the popular vote by over half a million votes but lost the Electoral College. This moment defined a generation of political tension between Republicans and Democrats and raised deep questions about the fairness of the American electoral system.

George W. Bush was a Republican from Texas, the son of former president George H. W. Bush. He ran on a platform of compassionate conservatism, promising to cut taxes, improve education, and maintain a humble foreign policy. His vice president was Dick Cheney, a seasoned political operator who had served in multiple Republican administrations. Bush won the Electoral College with 271 votes to Gore's 266, the narrowest possible margin at the time. Democrats were furious, believing that the Supreme Court had stolen the election from their candidate. The bitterness of the 2000 election set the tone for the partisan warfare that would define American politics for the next two decades.

Everything changed on September 11, 2001, when al-Qaeda terrorists hijacked four commercial airplanes and crashed them into the World Trade Center in New York City, the Pentagon in Virginia, and a field in Pennsylvania. Nearly three thousand people were killed, making it the deadliest attack on American soil in history. Bush's presidency was immediately transformed from a domestic policy agenda into a war presidency. He addressed the nation with resolve and declared a global war on terror. His approval ratings surged to nearly ninety percent in the days following the attacks, a level of national unity that had not been seen since World War Two.

Bush responded to the September 11 attacks by launching the War in Afghanistan in October 2001, targeting the Taliban regime that had harbored Osama bin Laden and al-Qaeda. Congress authorized the use of military force with near unanimous support, including support from Democrats. The initial invasion toppled the Taliban government quickly, but the war dragged on for two decades and became America's longest war. Democrats who had initially supported the war grew increasingly critical as casualties mounted and no clear path to victory emerged. The war in Afghanistan would haunt American politics for generations, with Bush, Obama, Trump, and Biden all grappling with its consequences.

In 2003, Bush made the fateful decision to invade Iraq, arguing that Iraqi dictator Saddam Hussein possessed weapons of mass destruction and posed a direct threat to the United States and its allies. The intelligence supporting this claim turned out to be wrong, and no weapons of mass destruction were ever found. Democrats who had supported the Iraq War, including senators like John Kerry, Hillary Clinton, and Joe Biden, later faced political consequences for those votes. The Iraq War became a defining issue of the 2004 presidential election, with Democratic candidate John Kerry making it the centerpiece of his challenge to Bush.

John Kerry, a Democratic senator from Massachusetts and a decorated Vietnam War veteran, won the Democratic nomination in 2004 after a competitive primary. Kerry argued that Bush had misled the country into an unnecessary war and was mismanaging the occupation of Iraq. Bush countered by portraying Kerry as a flip-flopper who had voted for the war before voting against funding it. A group called Swift Boat Veterans for Truth ran ads attacking Kerry's war record, a move widely condemned by Democrats as a smear campaign. Bush won reelection with 286 electoral votes to Kerry's 251, carrying the popular vote for the first time with over 62 million votes.

Bush's second term was marked by two major crises that severely damaged his presidency. The first was Hurricane Katrina in August 2005, which devastated New Orleans and the Gulf Coast, killing over 1,800 people. The federal government's slow and disorganized response to the disaster was seen as a catastrophic failure. Bush's comment that FEMA director Michael Brown was doing a heck of a job became a symbol of government incompetence and racial inequality, as the people left behind were disproportionately poor and Black. Democrats hammered Bush for the Katrina response, and his approval ratings never fully recovered.

The second crisis was the financial collapse of 2008, which began with the bursting of the housing bubble and the collapse of the subprime mortgage market. Major financial institutions including Lehman Brothers, Bear Stearns, and AIG were either allowed to fail or required massive government bailouts. The economy shed hundreds of thousands of jobs per month, and the stock market lost nearly half its value. Bush signed the Troubled Asset Relief Program, a seven hundred billion dollar bailout of the financial sector that was deeply unpopular with the American public. The financial crisis created the opening for Barack Obama to win the presidency, as voters blamed Republicans for the economic catastrophe.

Barack Obama's rise was one of the most remarkable stories in American political history. He was a first-term Democratic senator from Illinois who had served only a few years in the Senate when he announced his presidential campaign in February 2007. Obama was African American, the son of a Kenyan father and a white mother from Kansas, and he represented a new generation of Democratic politics. His primary campaign against Hillary Clinton was one of the most competitive and historic in Democratic Party history. Clinton was the heavy favorite, backed by the entire Democratic establishment and the Clinton political machine. Obama won by building a grassroots coalition of young voters, African Americans, and college-educated whites.

Hillary Clinton was a former first lady, the wife of President Bill Clinton, and a senator from New York. She was the first woman to mount a serious presidential campaign in American history, and many Democrats believed it was her time. The primary battle between Obama and Clinton was intensely personal and sometimes bitter, with accusations of race-baiting on one side and gender bias on the other. Obama ultimately won the nomination through his superior ground game and ability to win delegates in caucus states. Clinton's supporters were initially devastated, but most eventually rallied behind Obama in the general election against Republican nominee John McCain.

John McCain was a Republican senator from Arizona and a genuine American war hero who had been a prisoner of war in Vietnam for more than five years. McCain was known as a maverick who sometimes broke with his party, and he had built a reputation for bipartisanship and independence. His campaign was hampered by the financial crisis and his association with the deeply unpopular Bush administration. McCain made the controversial choice of Alaska Governor Sarah Palin as his running mate, a decision that energized the Republican base but raised serious questions about presidential judgment. Palin's lack of foreign policy knowledge and her sometimes chaotic interviews with journalists became a liability for the campaign.

Obama won the 2008 presidential election decisively, carrying 365 electoral votes to McCain's 173 and winning the popular vote by over seven percentage points. Obama's victory was celebrated around the world as a historic breakthrough, as he became the first African American president in United States history. Democrats won large majorities in both the House and Senate, giving Obama a mandate to pursue his ambitious domestic agenda. The joy of Obama's election was tempered by the severity of the economic crisis he inherited, with the economy losing nearly eight hundred thousand jobs in the month before he took office.

Obama's presidency began with the dual challenge of ending the financial crisis and reforming the American healthcare system. His economic team crafted the American Recovery and Reinvestment Act, an eight hundred and thirty billion dollar stimulus package that Democrats argued saved the economy from a second Great Depression. Republicans almost unanimously opposed the stimulus, arguing it was wasteful spending that would saddle future generations with debt. The partisan divide over the stimulus set the pattern for Obama's entire first term, as Republicans in Congress united in opposition to his agenda and refused to work across the aisle.

The Affordable Care Act, also known as Obamacare, was Obama's signature domestic achievement. The law expanded health insurance coverage to tens of millions of Americans who had previously been uninsured, required insurance companies to cover people with pre-existing conditions, and allowed young adults to remain on their parents' insurance until age 26. Democrats celebrated the law as the most significant expansion of the social safety net since Lyndon Johnson's Great Society programs of the 1960s. Republicans vowed to repeal it from the moment it was signed into law, calling it a government takeover of healthcare and an unconstitutional mandate on individuals to purchase insurance.

The Republican opposition to Obama was fierce and sometimes personal. The Tea Party movement emerged in 2009 and 2010 as a grassroots revolt against Obama's policies, particularly the stimulus and the healthcare law. Tea Party activists were predominantly white, older, and deeply conservative, and they channeled their anger into the 2010 midterm elections. Republicans gained 63 seats in the House, the largest midterm gain by any party since 1938, and recaptured the House majority. Speaker John Boehner and the new Republican majority pledged to stop Obama's agenda and make him a one-term president.

Despite the Republican obstruction, Obama secured several significant achievements during his first term. He oversaw the end of combat operations in Iraq, fulfilling a campaign promise to bring American troops home. On May 2, 2011, Navy SEAL Team Six raided a compound in Abbottabad, Pakistan, and killed Osama bin Laden, the architect of the September 11 attacks. The successful raid was a major victory for Obama and momentarily united the country across party lines. Obama and his national security team watched the raid in real time from the White House Situation Room in one of the most dramatic moments of his presidency.

Obama ran for reelection in 2012 against Republican nominee Mitt Romney, a wealthy former governor of Massachusetts who had previously run for president in 2008. Romney was a moderate Republican who had actually implemented a healthcare reform law in Massachusetts that closely resembled the Affordable Care Act. Obama attacked Romney as an out-of-touch plutocrat whose business career at Bain Capital had involved laying off American workers. Romney attacked Obama as a failed president who had made the economy worse and burdened the country with unaffordable healthcare mandates. Obama won reelection with 332 electoral votes to Romney's 206, and won the popular vote by nearly four percentage points.

Obama's second term was complicated by foreign policy crises and continued Republican obstruction. The rise of the Islamic State in Iraq and Syria forced Obama to recommit American military forces to the region he had promised to leave. Russian President Vladimir Putin's annexation of Crimea in 2014 created the most serious confrontation between the United States and Russia since the Cold War. Democrats generally backed Obama's cautious response to these crises, while Republicans accused him of weakness and leading from behind. The Republican-controlled Senate refused to confirm many of Obama's judicial nominees, including his nominee to the Supreme Court, Merrick Garland, after Justice Antonin Scalia died in February 2016.

The refusal to consider Merrick Garland's nomination was one of the most controversial acts of Republican Senate leadership under Mitch McConnell. McConnell argued that no Supreme Court nomination should be confirmed in an election year, citing a precedent that Democrats said he had invented. Democrats were furious, arguing that the Constitution required the Senate to consider presidential nominations regardless of the political calendar. The stolen Supreme Court seat became a rallying point for Democrats and a source of immense pride for Republicans, who believed they had masterfully outmaneuvered Obama on one of the most consequential decisions a president can make.

The 2016 presidential election was one of the most shocking and consequential in American history. Hillary Clinton, who had run unsuccessfully against Obama in 2008, ran again for the Democratic nomination and was seen as the inevitable candidate. Her primary opponent was Bernie Sanders, a democratic socialist senator from Vermont who had never been a member of the Democratic Party. Sanders' insurgent campaign attracted enormous enthusiasm from young voters and progressives who felt that Clinton was too closely tied to Wall Street and the political establishment. Clinton won the nomination, but Sanders' campaign revealed deep divisions within the Democratic Party between its establishment and progressive wings.

Donald Trump, a New York real estate developer and reality television star, entered the Republican primary as a long-shot candidate in June 2015. Trump's announcement speech was immediately controversial, as he called Mexican immigrants criminals and rapists and promised to build a wall on the southern border and make Mexico pay for it. Republican leaders and commentators initially dismissed Trump as a joke candidate, but he proved them wrong at every turn. Trump's populist message of putting America first, his attacks on free trade agreements, and his willingness to say things that mainstream politicians would never say resonated with a significant portion of the Republican base.

Trump defeated sixteen other Republican candidates in the primary, dispatching establishment favorites like Jeb Bush, Marco Rubio, and Ted Cruz with a combination of personal attacks, populist rhetoric, and media savvy. He called Jeb Bush low energy, Marco Rubio Little Marco, and Ted Cruz Lyin' Ted, nicknames that stuck and defined his opponents in the minds of Republican primary voters. Trump's domination of cable news coverage gave him an enormous advantage, as networks found that his appearances and controversies drew massive audiences. The Republican establishment was unable to stop him, and many reluctantly fell in line behind him once he secured the nomination.

The general election between Trump and Clinton was among the nastiest and most divisive in modern history. Clinton attacked Trump as unfit for the presidency, pointing to his lack of government experience, his history of business failures and lawsuits, and his inflammatory statements about women, immigrants, and minorities. Trump attacked Clinton as corrupt and dishonest, reviving scandals from the Clinton years and hammering her use of a private email server as secretary of state. The FBI's investigation into Clinton's email server, and director James Comey's announcement eleven days before the election that he was reopening the investigation, may have tipped the balance in Trump's favor.

Trump won the 2016 election with 306 electoral votes, carrying critical Rust Belt states including Pennsylvania, Michigan, and Wisconsin that had voted Democratic in every presidential election since the 1980s. Clinton won the popular vote by nearly three million votes, the largest popular vote margin in history for a losing candidate. Democrats were devastated, and many struggled to explain how their supposedly inevitable candidate had lost to a man they considered manifestly unqualified. Trump's victory was celebrated by his supporters as a revolution against the political establishment and condemned by his opponents as a catastrophe for American democracy.

Trump's presidency was unlike anything the United States had seen in modern times. He governed through Twitter, bypassing traditional media and communicating directly with his supporters. He attacked the press as the enemy of the people, undermined the credibility of the intelligence community, and feuded publicly with allies and opponents alike. His Republican allies in Congress largely supported him despite his unconventional behavior, understanding that his base was loyal to him and that crossing him could mean political death at the primary stage. Democrats were in constant opposition, and the resistance movement that emerged after Trump's election mobilized millions of Americans into political activism.

Trump's policy achievements included a massive tax cut that primarily benefited corporations and wealthy individuals, the appointment of three conservative Supreme Court justices, and the renegotiation of the North American Free Trade Agreement into the United States Mexico Canada Agreement. His appointees to the federal judiciary were uniformly conservative, reshaping the courts in ways that would influence American law for a generation. Republicans celebrated these achievements as a generational transformation of American policy and jurisprudence. Democrats argued that the tax cuts blew up the deficit, the trade renegotiation was mostly cosmetic, and the judicial appointments were the product of Republican norm-breaking on the Merrick Garland nomination.

Trump's tenure was also marked by scandal, investigations, and two historic impeachments. Special Counsel Robert Mueller's investigation into Russian interference in the 2016 election consumed the first two years of Trump's presidency. Mueller's report documented extensive Russian efforts to help Trump win the election and numerous instances of potential obstruction of justice by Trump. Democrats argued the report was a roadmap for impeachment. Trump and his Republican allies declared total exoneration and attacked the Mueller investigation as a partisan witch hunt. The investigation deepened the partisan divide and left both sides unable to agree on even the basic facts of what had happened.

Trump was first impeached by the Democratic-controlled House in December 2019 on charges of abuse of power and obstruction of Congress related to his attempt to pressure Ukraine to investigate Joe Biden and his son Hunter Biden. Trump had withheld congressionally approved military aid to Ukraine as leverage, a quid pro quo that Democrats argued was a clear abuse of presidential power. Every Republican in the House voted against impeachment, and the Republican-controlled Senate acquitted Trump in February 2020, also along strict party lines. The impeachment deepened the partisan divisions in the country and raised fundamental questions about whether Congress could effectively check a president of the same party.

The COVID-19 pandemic struck the United States in early 2020, becoming the defining crisis of Trump's presidency. The virus spread rapidly across the country, eventually killing more than a million Americans. Trump's response to the pandemic was widely criticized by Democrats and public health experts, who argued that he had downplayed the threat, politicized public health measures, and failed to provide national leadership. Trump promoted unproven treatments, questioned the guidance of his own public health officials, and resisted mask mandates. Republicans generally supported Trump's approach, arguing that the economic costs of lockdowns were too severe and that individual freedom should take precedence over government mandates.

The pandemic became intensely politicized, with Democrats generally supporting masks, social distancing, and eventual vaccine mandates, while Republicans generally opposed government restrictions. Trump deserves credit for Operation Warp Speed, the public-private partnership that produced multiple effective COVID-19 vaccines in record time. The vaccines were authorized before the end of 2020, an extraordinary scientific achievement that Trump rightly claimed as a success of his administration. However, Trump's mixed messages about vaccines and his own reluctance to promote them consistently undermined vaccine uptake among his supporters and complicated the public health response.

Joe Biden ran for president in 2020 for the third time, having previously run in 1988 and 2008 without success. Biden had served for 36 years as a senator from Delaware and for eight years as Obama's vice president. He positioned himself as a unifying figure who could restore decency and competence to the White House after four years of Trump. Biden's primary campaign was rocky, and he struggled in the early states before a decisive victory in South Carolina propelled him to the nomination. The South Carolina victory was powered largely by African American voters, who trusted Biden due to his association with Obama and his long record of civil rights support.

Biden's choice of Kamala Harris as his running mate was historic in multiple ways. Harris was the first woman, the first Black American, and the first person of South Asian descent to be nominated for vice president by a major party. Harris had previously run for the Democratic presidential nomination herself and had been a senator from California and the state's attorney general. Her selection was celebrated by Democrats as a historic breakthrough and a signal that the party was committed to diversity and inclusion. Republicans attacked Harris as the most liberal member of the Senate and argued that Biden had been captured by the progressive wing of his party.

Biden won the 2020 election with 306 electoral votes, the same number Trump had won in 2016. Biden won the popular vote by more than seven million votes, the largest popular vote margin in decades. Biden flipped the critical states of Pennsylvania, Michigan, Wisconsin, Georgia, and Arizona that Trump had won in 2016. Trump and his allies immediately challenged the results, claiming without evidence that the election had been stolen through widespread voter fraud. These claims were rejected by federal courts in over sixty lawsuits, by Republican-appointed judges, and by Trump's own attorney general, William Barr, who declared that there was no evidence of fraud sufficient to change the outcome.

Trump's refusal to concede the 2020 election and his promotion of the stolen election narrative set the stage for the January 6, 2021 attack on the United States Capitol. On that day, Trump addressed a crowd of tens of thousands of his supporters and urged them to march to the Capitol and fight like hell. A mob stormed the Capitol building while Congress was in the process of certifying Biden's electoral victory, temporarily disrupting the proceeding and forcing lawmakers to take shelter. Five people died in connection with the attack, and hundreds of Capitol Police officers were injured. The attack was the most serious assault on American democracy in modern history and a dark chapter in the story of American political violence.

Trump was impeached a second time by the House for incitement of insurrection following the January 6 attack, making him the only president in American history to be impeached twice. The Senate again acquitted him, with most Republicans arguing that impeaching a former president was unconstitutional. Seven Republican senators voted to convict, the most bipartisan vote on a Trump impeachment. Democrats argued that the acquittal sent a dangerous message that presidents could incite violence without facing accountability. The events of January 6 became a defining issue of American political life, with Republicans and Democrats unable to agree on what had happened or who was responsible.

Joe Biden was inaugurated as the 46th president of the United States on January 20, 2021, against the backdrop of the COVID-19 pandemic and the aftermath of the January 6 attack. Biden delivered an inaugural address calling for unity and healing and promised to be a president for all Americans, including those who had not voted for him. His administration moved quickly to rejoin the Paris Climate Agreement and the World Health Organization, reversing Trump's decisions to withdraw from both. Biden signed a series of executive orders reversing many of Trump's immigration policies, including the travel ban on majority-Muslim countries and the family separation policy at the border.

Biden's first major legislative achievement was the American Rescue Plan, a one point nine trillion dollar COVID-19 relief package that provided direct payments to most Americans, extended enhanced unemployment benefits, and funded vaccine distribution. The law passed with no Republican votes, as Republicans argued it was too large and included too much spending unrelated to COVID-19. Democrats argued the scale of the response was necessary given the severity of the economic crisis. The American Rescue Plan also included the expanded Child Tax Credit, which temporarily cut child poverty in America nearly in half before it expired at the end of 2021 when Congress failed to renew it.

Biden also achieved passage of the bipartisan Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act in November 2021, a one point two trillion dollar investment in roads, bridges, broadband internet, clean water, and public transit. The law was the largest infrastructure investment in American history and was supported by a significant number of Republican senators and House members. Biden argued that the law showed that bipartisanship was still possible in Washington, while progressives in his party criticized him for compromising too much to win Republican votes. The infrastructure law was a genuine achievement that Biden rightfully celebrated as proof that government could still work.

The withdrawal from Afghanistan in August 2021 was the most damaging episode of Biden's presidency. After twenty years of war, Biden decided to follow through on Trump's agreement with the Taliban and withdraw all American troops. The withdrawal was chaotic, and the Afghan government collapsed far more quickly than American intelligence had predicted. The Taliban retook the country within days, and scenes of desperate Afghans clinging to departing American military aircraft shocked the world. Biden defended the decision to end the war but acknowledged that the execution could have been better. Republicans attacked the withdrawal as a catastrophic failure that damaged American credibility and emboldened adversaries.

Kamala Harris served as vice president with a portfolio that included leading the administration's response to the root causes of migration from Central America and protecting voting rights. Her efforts on both issues were criticized as insufficient, and her approval ratings fell below even Biden's historically low numbers. Harris was seen by some Democrats as failing to live up to the historic nature of her role, while others argued she was being held to an unfair standard because of her race and gender. Republicans targeted Harris relentlessly, portraying her as incompetent and suggesting she was waiting to take over from a diminished president.

Biden's legislative agenda ran into serious trouble in late 2021 when Democratic senators Joe Manchin of West Virginia and Kyrsten Sinema of Arizona refused to support the Build Back Better Act, a multi-trillion dollar social spending bill that would have expanded Medicare, addressed climate change, provided universal pre-K education, and paid for these investments with tax increases on corporations and wealthy Americans. Manchin's opposition was rooted in his concerns about inflation and the deficit, while Sinema's concerns were more focused on the tax increases. The failure to pass Build Back Better was a significant blow to the progressive wing of the Democratic Party and to Biden's ambitions to be a transformative president.

Despite this setback, Biden and Democrats eventually passed the Inflation Reduction Act in August 2022, a scaled-back version of Build Back Better that included significant climate investments, prescription drug pricing reforms, and some tax increases on corporations. The law was the largest climate legislation in American history, allocating nearly four hundred billion dollars to clean energy and climate programs. It passed with no Republican votes, with all Republicans arguing it would raise taxes, increase inflation, and harm the economy. Democrats hailed it as a historic achievement and evidence that their party was serious about addressing climate change.

The 2022 midterm elections defied historical expectations. The party of a first-term president typically loses significant numbers of seats in midterm elections, and with Biden's low approval ratings and high inflation, Republicans were widely expected to win large majorities in both chambers. Republicans did win control of the House, but by a much narrower margin than expected. Democrats actually gained seats in the Senate, a remarkable outcome that was attributed in part to the Supreme Court's June 2022 decision to overturn Roe v. Wade and eliminate the constitutional right to abortion. The Dobbs decision, written by the justices appointed by Trump, mobilized Democratic voters and suburban women who had moved toward Republicans but came back to Democrats over abortion rights.

Trump announced his candidacy for the 2024 presidential election in November 2022, just days after the midterms that disappointed Republicans. His announcement was seen as an attempt to get ahead of any potential criminal indictments and to dominate the Republican field before potential rivals could establish themselves. Trump was the clear frontrunner for the Republican nomination from the moment he announced, based on his continued hold on the Republican base. His rivals, including Governor Ron DeSantis of Florida, Senator Tim Scott of South Carolina, and former Governor Nikki Haley of South Carolina, struggled to break through.

Trump faced multiple criminal indictments in 2023 and 2024, including federal charges related to his handling of classified documents, federal charges related to his efforts to overturn the 2020 election, and state charges in Georgia related to his alleged conspiracy to steal that state's presidential electors. Democrats argued the indictments were necessary to hold a powerful former president accountable for his actions. Trump and Republicans argued the indictments were politically motivated persecution designed to stop him from running for president. The indictments actually boosted Trump's standing in the Republican primary, as his supporters rallied around him and his fundraising surged each time he was charged.

Biden announced his reelection campaign in April 2023, arguing that the choice between himself and Trump was a battle for the soul of America. Biden's age was a constant issue, as he was already the oldest person ever to serve as president, and he would be 82 years old at the end of a second term. Questions about his cognitive fitness intensified following a disastrous debate performance against Trump in June 2024, in which Biden appeared confused, spoke haltingly, and failed to effectively prosecute the case against Trump. Democrats were alarmed by the performance, and pressure mounted for Biden to withdraw from the race.

Biden announced in July 2024 that he was withdrawing from the 2024 presidential election and endorsing Kamala Harris as the Democratic nominee. This was an unprecedented decision, as no incumbent president had ever withdrawn from the race so late in the electoral calendar. Biden's withdrawal was celebrated by Democrats who feared he could not win, and Harris quickly consolidated support from across the party. Harris chose Minnesota Governor Tim Walz as her running mate, a folksy Midwestern progressive who was intended to appeal to working-class white voters in the Rust Belt states that Democrats needed to win.

The 2024 presidential election between Donald Trump and Kamala Harris was one of the most consequential in American history. Harris campaigned on protecting abortion rights, strengthening democracy, and building on the economic achievements of the Biden administration. Trump campaigned on immigration restriction, tariffs on imported goods, ending the war in Ukraine through a quick deal with Russia, and retribution against his political enemies. Harris was the first woman of color to be the presidential nominee of a major party. Trump was seeking to become the first person in American history to win the presidency after being convicted of a felony, as he had been convicted in New York on 34 counts of falsifying business records.

Trump won the 2024 presidential election, defeating Kamala Harris by winning all of the key battleground states including Pennsylvania, Michigan, Wisconsin, Georgia, Arizona, and Nevada. Trump became the first president to serve non-consecutive terms since Grover Cleveland in the 19th century. He also became the first convicted felon to be elected president of the United States. Republicans won control of the Senate and narrowly held the House, giving Trump a unified government to work with. Democrats were devastated by the loss, with many pointing to Biden's late withdrawal, economic anxiety about inflation, and concerns about immigration as key factors in the defeat.

The Republican Party under Trump transformed from the party of Ronald Reagan, which championed free trade, international alliances, and limited government, into a populist nationalist party that was skeptical of free trade, critical of NATO allies, and willing to use government power to achieve its goals. Trump's influence over the Republican Party was total, and Republicans who crossed him faced primary challenges and political oblivion. The transformation of the Republican Party under Trump was one of the most significant developments in American political history and fundamentally changed the nature of the partisan divide in the country.

The Democratic Party under Obama and Biden was similarly transformed, moving significantly to the left on issues like healthcare, climate change, immigration, and criminal justice. The progressive wing of the party, represented by figures like Bernie Sanders, Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez, and Elizabeth Warren, pushed the party toward more ambitious policy proposals and a more confrontational posture toward corporations and wealthy interests. Moderate Democrats like Biden argued that the party needed to focus on winning back working-class voters who had defected to Trump. The tension between the progressive and moderate wings of the Democratic Party was a constant source of internal conflict that complicated the party's ability to govern and to win elections.

The story of American presidential politics in the 21st century is fundamentally a story of deepening partisan polarization. George W. Bush was able to win with 50.7 percent of the popular vote in 2004, but he was deeply divisive. Obama was elected with hope and promise of unity but governed in an era of intense opposition. Trump brought polarization to new heights, governing in a way that was explicitly designed to energize his base while dismissing the concerns of his opponents. Biden tried to restore normalcy and bipartisanship but found that the structural forces driving polarization were too powerful to overcome through goodwill alone.

The Electoral College has become a central battleground in American presidential politics, with both parties focused on winning the same handful of competitive states. Pennsylvania, Michigan, Wisconsin, Arizona, Georgia, and Nevada have determined the outcome of recent elections, while large states like California, New York, and Texas are considered safely Democratic or Republican. The focus on swing states means that presidential campaigns spend almost all of their time and resources in a small number of places, arguably distorting national priorities. Democrats have repeatedly called for abolishing the Electoral College in favor of a national popular vote, while Republicans have defended it as a constitutional protection for smaller states.

The role of money in presidential politics has exploded in the 21st century, following Supreme Court decisions like Citizens United in 2010, which held that corporations and other groups have a First Amendment right to spend unlimited money on political advertising. The Citizens United decision was celebrated by Republicans as a victory for free speech and condemned by Democrats as a corruption of democracy. Super PACs funded by billionaires on both sides spent billions of dollars in presidential elections, amplifying their voices at the expense of ordinary voters. Trump proved that a candidate could also fund much of his campaign through small dollar donations from a large base of passionate supporters.

The media landscape in which presidential politics is conducted has transformed dramatically since 2000. The rise of social media, particularly Facebook and Twitter, allowed politicians to communicate directly with supporters without the filter of traditional journalism. Fox News became the dominant voice of conservative media and an essential platform for Republican politicians, while MSNBC became the preferred network of liberal viewers. The fragmentation of media meant that Americans increasingly received news from sources that confirmed their existing beliefs, contributing to the political polarization that defined the era. Trump used Twitter with particular effectiveness, driving news cycles and communicating with his base in ways that traditional media could not ignore.

The relationship between the Republican and Democratic parties on issues of presidential conduct and constitutional norms changed dramatically during this period. Democrats impeached Trump twice, arguing that his behavior represented fundamental violations of the presidential oath. Republicans largely defended Trump against both impeachments, arguing that the charges were politically motivated and did not meet the constitutional standard for removal from office. When Bill Clinton was impeached by Republicans in 1998 for lying under oath about an affair, Democrats had similarly rallied around their president. The partisan use of the impeachment power raised questions about whether the mechanism could ever again serve its intended function as a check on presidential misconduct.

George W. Bush left office in January 2009 with an approval rating of around 22 percent, one of the lowest of any departing president in history. The combination of the Iraq War, Hurricane Katrina, and the financial crisis had severely damaged his standing. In the years that followed, particularly after Trump's election, Bush's reputation was somewhat rehabilitated among Democrats, who came to see him as a more normal and acceptable form of Republican compared to Trump. Bush spoke out occasionally against Trump's rhetoric on immigration and democratic norms, though he never endorsed Trump's Democratic opponents. The rehabilitation of Bush's legacy among some Democrats illustrated how Trump had shifted the frame of reference for what was considered acceptable presidential behavior.

Obama left office in January 2017 with an approval rating of around 60 percent, unusually high for a departing president. His presidency had been hampered by Republican obstruction but had nonetheless produced significant achievements in healthcare, economic recovery, and foreign policy. Obama was succeeded by Trump, whose election represented a dramatic repudiation of Obama's legacy and a rejection of the demographic and cultural changes that Obama embodied. The contrast between Obama and Trump was stark in almost every dimension, from their personal styles and rhetoric to their policy priorities and governing philosophies. The swing from Obama to Trump and back to Biden suggested a country deeply divided about its identity and direction.

The question of presidential character became increasingly central to American politics during this period. Democrats argued that Trump's personal behavior, including his documented dishonesty, his treatment of women, and his inflammatory rhetoric about immigrants and minorities, was fundamentally disqualifying for the office of president. Republicans argued that Trump's character flaws were irrelevant compared to his policy achievements, and that the media held Trump to an unfair standard while ignoring the flaws of Democratic presidents. The debate about presidential character reflected deeper disagreements about the nature of the office and what qualities Americans should demand of their leaders.

The immigration debate was one of the most contentious issues of 21st century American presidential politics. Bush sought comprehensive immigration reform that would have created a path to citizenship for undocumented immigrants, but the effort failed in Congress because of opposition from the Republican base. Obama similarly sought comprehensive immigration reform and when Congress failed to act, he used executive authority to protect certain undocumented immigrants from deportation through the Deferred Action for Childhood Arrivals program. Trump made immigration restriction the centerpiece of his presidency, attempting to build a wall on the southern border, drastically reducing legal immigration, and implementing harsh enforcement measures. Biden reversed many of Trump's immigration policies but struggled with a surge in border crossings that became a political liability.

The relationship between the president and the Supreme Court has been enormously consequential in this period. Bush appointed John Roberts as chief justice and Samuel Alito as associate justice, solidifying a conservative majority on the Court. Obama was blocked from appointing Merrick Garland, and his two successful appointments of Sonia Sotomayor and Elena Kagan did not change the ideological balance of the Court. Trump appointed Neil Gorsuch to the seat that had been held open for him by Senate Republicans, Brett Kavanaugh to replace the retiring Anthony Kennedy, and Amy Coney Barrett to replace Ruth Bader Ginsburg just weeks before the 2020 election. The three Trump appointees formed part of the six-justice conservative majority that overturned Roe v. Wade in 2022.

The overturning of Roe v. Wade was one of the most consequential Supreme Court decisions in American history and fundamentally changed the politics of abortion. For nearly fifty years, the constitutional right to abortion established by Roe had been the law of the land, and Republican opposition to abortion had been primarily expressed through attempts to restrict or regulate the procedure rather than ban it outright. When the conservative Court overturned Roe, it returned the question of abortion regulation to individual states, immediately triggering bans and severe restrictions in Republican-controlled states. The Dobbs decision galvanized Democratic voters and proved to be a major political liability for Republicans in multiple election cycles.

The pandemic politics of 2020 and 2021 exposed deep divisions between Republican and Democratic approaches to government and public health. Democratic governors generally implemented stricter public health measures, mask mandates, and vaccination requirements, while Republican governors like Ron DeSantis of Florida and Greg Abbott of Texas emphasized individual freedom and opposed mandates. The partisan divide over COVID-19 response was stunning in its completeness, with views on masking, vaccination, and school closures almost perfectly correlated with political party identification. The pandemic accelerated existing political trends, driving college-educated voters further toward Democrats and working-class voters further toward Republicans.

Ron DeSantis emerged as a significant figure in Republican politics during the COVID-19 pandemic, positioning himself as the alternative to both the lockdown approach of blue-state governors and the erratic leadership of Trump. DeSantis won reelection as governor of Florida by nearly twenty percentage points in 2022, a result that led many Republicans and commentators to believe he would be a formidable candidate for the 2024 presidential nomination. DeSantis positioned himself as Trump without the drama, promising all of the policy outcomes of Trumpism without the chaos and self-destructiveness. His campaign ultimately failed to break through against Trump's dominance of the Republican base, and he dropped out before the Iowa caucuses.

The 2024 Republican primary illustrated Trump's total command of the Republican Party. Despite facing criminal indictments on federal and state charges, Trump won every single primary and caucus by large margins. His rivals attempted various strategies to stop him, including attacking him directly, refusing to attack him out of fear of his supporters, and positioning themselves as the choice when he inevitably collapsed. None of these strategies worked, and Trump secured the nomination earlier and more decisively than in either of his previous primary campaigns. The willingness of Republican voters to nominate a candidate facing 91 criminal charges was a testament to their devotion to Trump and their disillusionment with the political and legal establishment.

Nikki Haley, the former governor of South Carolina and Trump's own ambassador to the United Nations, proved to be Trump's most durable primary challenger. She ran as a moderate alternative who could win back the suburban voters who had defected to Democrats over Trump. Haley won significant support in New Hampshire and other states with large numbers of college-educated Republicans, demonstrating that there was a constituency for a post-Trump Republican Party. But she could not overcome Trump's grip on the base, and she eventually suspended her campaign while pointedly refusing to immediately endorse Trump, suggesting she was positioning herself for 2028.

The relationship between the two major parties in this era was characterized by a combination of intense policy disagreement and personal animosity that made governance increasingly difficult. The Senate filibuster, which requires 60 votes to pass most legislation, became a weapon used by both parties to block the agenda of the other. Democrats used the filibuster to block Bush's Social Security privatization plan, and Republicans used it to block most of Obama's and Biden's ambitious domestic proposals. Both parties periodically threatened to eliminate the filibuster but backed down from doing so, recognizing that they would someday be in the minority again and would want the protection it provided.

The increasing role of executive action in American governance reflected the legislative gridlock that resulted from partisan polarization. Unable to pass major legislation through Congress, presidents increasingly relied on executive orders, regulatory changes, and administrative actions to advance their agendas. Obama used executive action on immigration, climate change, and other issues when Congress failed to act. Trump used executive action to undo much of what Obama had done and to advance his own priorities on immigration and regulatory policy. Biden used executive action to reverse Trump's actions and to pursue his own agenda on climate and other issues. The cycle of executive action and reversal raised fundamental questions about the durability of policies made without legislative consensus.

The politics of race have been central to the presidential politics of this era in ways both explicit and implicit. Obama's election as the first Black president was a historic milestone, but his presidency also provoked a backlash among white voters who felt that the changing demographics and culture of America were leaving them behind. Trump's campaign explicitly tapped into these anxieties, with his birther conspiracy theory questioning Obama's American birth, his attacks on Latino immigrants, and his initial reluctance to disavow support from white supremacists. The Republican coalition under Trump was increasingly dependent on white voters without college degrees, while the Democratic coalition became more dependent on voters of color and college-educated whites.

The question of voter access and election administration became intensely contested during this period. Republicans pushed voter identification laws, restrictions on early voting, and purges of voter rolls, arguing these measures were necessary to prevent fraud. Democrats argued these measures were designed to suppress the votes of minority, young, and lower-income voters who tend to support Democrats. The Supreme Court's 2013 decision in Shelby County v. Holder gutted the Voting Rights Act, eliminating the requirement that states with a history of discrimination get federal approval before changing their voting laws. Following the 2020 election, Republican-controlled states passed dozens of laws making it harder to vote, which Democrats called the most serious assault on voting rights since the Jim Crow era.

Biden's presidency also grappled with significant foreign policy challenges beyond Afghanistan. Russia's full-scale invasion of Ukraine in February 2022 was the most serious European military conflict since World War Two, and Biden responded by rallying NATO allies to impose severe economic sanctions on Russia and provide substantial military assistance to Ukraine. The bipartisan consensus in favor of supporting Ukraine was strong initially, but support among Republicans began to wane as Trump and other Republican leaders questioned why the United States was spending billions of dollars on a war in Europe. The debate over Ukraine assistance revealed the transformation of Republican foreign policy from hawkish internationalism to the America-first skepticism that Trump had pioneered.

The legacy of the 21st century American presidency is complex and contested. George W. Bush launched two wars that defined a generation and failed to prevent the worst financial crisis since the Great Depression, but he also addressed the AIDS crisis in Africa, expanded Medicare prescription drug coverage, and maintained a respectful tone in public life. Obama restored economic stability after the financial crisis, expanded healthcare access, and killed Osama bin Laden, but he also expanded drone warfare, deported more immigrants than any previous president, and failed to close the Guantanamo detention facility. Trump cut taxes, appointed conservative judges, and achieved a more secure southern border, but he also presided over a chaotic pandemic response, two impeachments, and an attack on the Capitol. Biden passed significant legislation on infrastructure, climate, and healthcare, but struggled with inflation, immigration, and questions about his fitness for office.

Presidential elections in America are not just contests between two candidates. They are referenda on the direction of the country, reflections of the anxieties and aspirations of the electorate, and battles between competing visions of what America is and should be. The elections of 2000, 2004, 2008, 2012, 2016, 2020, and 2024 were each unique in their particulars but all part of the same ongoing contest over American identity. Republicans have consistently argued for a vision of America that emphasizes individual freedom, limited government, strong borders, and traditional values. Democrats have consistently argued for a vision that emphasizes collective responsibility, active government, diversity and inclusion, and science-based policy. The elections of the 21st century have swung back and forth between these two visions without either achieving a durable majority.

The contested election of 2000 set a precedent that would haunt American democracy for years. For the first time in modern history, the losing candidate in the popular vote became president, and the result was determined not by voters but by a Supreme Court decision that was widely seen as partisan. Al Gore's concession, and his gracious acceptance of a result he privately believed was unjust, was widely praised as a display of civic virtue that put the good of the country above personal ambition. The contrast between Gore's concession in 2000 and Trump's refusal to concede in 2020 was stark, and it illuminated how much the norms of American democracy had eroded over two decades of partisan warfare.

The Bush administration's response to September 11 included not just military action but a dramatic expansion of domestic surveillance under the PATRIOT Act, which was passed with overwhelming bipartisan support in the weeks after the attacks. The National Security Agency's surveillance programs, revealed by whistleblower Edward Snowden in 2013, showed that the post-September 11 expansion of government surveillance had grown far beyond what most Americans understood or had consented to. Obama continued and in some cases expanded these surveillance programs even as he expressed reservations about them, illustrating the tension between civil liberties and national security that every modern president has had to navigate.

The debate about the use of torture in interrogating terrorism suspects was one of the most morally significant controversies of the Bush era. The Bush administration authorized enhanced interrogation techniques, including waterboarding, that most legal experts and human rights organizations considered torture. Obama banned these techniques on his first full day in office through an executive order. Democrats condemned the Bush administration's interrogation policies as illegal and immoral. Republicans, including Dick Cheney, defended them as necessary and effective in preventing further attacks. The Senate Intelligence Committee's investigation into the CIA's detention and interrogation program, released in 2014, concluded that the techniques had not produced the intelligence benefits the CIA had claimed.

Obama's healthcare reform was not just a policy achievement but a political transformation. Before the Affordable Care Act, Republicans had long argued for expanding access to healthcare through market mechanisms, and the framework of the ACA was originally developed by the Heritage Foundation, a conservative think tank, and implemented in Massachusetts by Mitt Romney. When Obama adopted this approach, Republicans almost unanimously turned against it, illustrating how what is politically acceptable depends on who is proposing it rather than the inherent merits of the policy. The ACA survived three Supreme Court challenges, two repeal attempts in Congress, and seven years of Republican promises to replace it with something better, demonstrating the durability of major social programs once they are enacted.

Trump's relationship with Republican members of Congress evolved over his time in office. Initially, many establishment Republicans were wary of Trump and tried to maintain their independence. Senators like John McCain, Bob Corker, and Jeff Flake were openly critical of Trump and refused to go along with all of his demands. But all three eventually left the Senate, either by death or by choosing not to seek reelection, and were replaced by more Trump-aligned Republicans. By the time of Trump's second term, virtually every Republican in Congress was an enthusiastic supporter, and the few who had crossed him had been defeated in primaries. The Republican Party had been remade in Trump's image.

The Democratic Party similarly went through a significant evolution during this period. The centrist Third Way politics of Bill Clinton, which had embraced free trade, welfare reform, and fiscal restraint, gave way to a more progressive politics under Obama, Hillary Clinton, and especially Bernie Sanders. The progressive movement within the Democratic Party pushed for Medicare for All, the Green New Deal, free college tuition, and other ambitious proposals that would have been considered far-left positions in the Clinton era. The tension between the Sanders-Warren progressive wing and the Biden-Pelosi moderate wing was a constant source of tension and drama that complicated the party's ability to present a unified front against Republicans.

Nancy Pelosi's role as House Speaker was one of the most powerful in the history of that position. Pelosi was the first woman to serve as Speaker, and she proved to be one of the most skilled legislative strategists of her era. She passed the Affordable Care Act, the American Rescue Plan, and other major legislation through narrow Democratic majorities, using a combination of negotiation, pressure, and political skill. She also led two impeachments of Donald Trump. Republicans despised her and made her a central target of their fundraising appeals. She retired from Democratic leadership after Republicans won the House majority in 2022.

Mitch McConnell's role as Senate Republican leader was similarly consequential, particularly his decisions on judicial nominations. McConnell's refusal to consider Merrick Garland's nomination in 2016 and his decision to change Senate rules to allow Supreme Court justices to be confirmed with a simple majority rather than 60 votes ensured that Trump's three nominees would be confirmed. McConnell's view of judicial appointments was purely strategic, focused on placing conservatives on the courts for decades regardless of the short-term political cost. Democrats argued that McConnell had fundamentally violated the constitutional norms governing the relationship between the president and the Senate on judicial nominations. Republicans argued he had simply played hardball politics and won.

The COVID-19 pandemic of 2020 and 2021 was not just a public health crisis but a stress test for American democratic institutions. Trump's efforts to pressure state officials to change their vote totals, his attacks on election officials who certified Biden's victory, and his promotion of conspiracy theories about voting machines were unprecedented in their directness and their threat to the peaceful transfer of power. Biden's transition team was initially denied the resources and access normally provided to an incoming administration, delaying the preparation for the new presidency. The ultimate peaceful transfer of power, despite the January 6 attack, demonstrated the resilience of American democratic institutions, though the attack also demonstrated how close those institutions had come to being disrupted.

The four presidents of the 21st century's first quarter each had defining characteristics that shaped their presidencies. Bush was defined by his religious faith, his commitment to the war on terror, and his ultimately tragic decision to invade Iraq. Obama was defined by his intelligence, his elegance, his historic identity as the first Black president, and his struggle to govern effectively in an era of unprecedented partisan obstruction. Trump was defined by his populism, his willingness to violate political norms, his contempt for traditional expertise, and his extraordinary hold on a devoted base of supporters. Biden was defined by his empathy, his long experience in government, his struggles with age, and his attempt to restore a more normal presidency after the Trump years.

The Republican and Democratic parties both face profound challenges as they navigate the turbulent politics of the contemporary era. The Republican Party under Trump has proven it can win presidential elections but has struggled to build the kind of durable majority that can transform the country over multiple electoral cycles. The Democratic Party has the demographic advantages of a growing coalition of voters of color and college-educated professionals but has struggled to connect with the working-class voters who once formed its base. Both parties are also dealing with internal tensions between their establishment and grassroots factions that complicate their ability to govern effectively when in power.

The future of American presidential politics will be shaped by forces that are already in motion. The growing diversity of the American electorate, as the proportion of non-white voters continues to increase, will put pressure on Republican strategies based on maximizing white voter turnout. The continuing economic anxieties of working-class Americans of all races will put pressure on Democratic strategies based on college-educated professionals. The accelerating climate crisis will force both parties to grapple with policies that are expensive and disruptive but potentially necessary to avoid catastrophic consequences. The rise of China as a peer competitor will require a sustained bipartisan effort to maintain American economic and military strength that has been difficult to achieve in the current polarized environment.

The Republican Party's evolution from the party of Reagan to the party of Trump was not a sudden transformation but a gradual process that had been underway for decades. Reagan had tapped into white working-class resentment in the 1980s with his attacks on welfare spending and his Southern strategy of appealing to racial anxieties. Newt Gingrich had pioneered the slash-and-burn politics of personal destruction in the 1990s with his relentless attacks on the Clinton administration. The Tea Party had demonstrated that the Republican base was hungry for confrontational anti-establishment politics. Trump simply took these existing trends to their logical conclusion, making explicit what had previously been implicit and abandoning the globalist economic policies that had always been more popular with Republican elites than with the Republican base.

The Democratic Party's failure to hold the working class was similarly a long-term process. The party of Roosevelt and Truman, which had built its majority around white working-class voters in the industrial Midwest and South, had been losing those voters since the civil rights revolution of the 1960s, when Lyndon Johnson famously predicted that by signing the Civil Rights Act he had handed the South to Republicans for a generation. Clinton had managed to hold some of these voters with his centrist economic message, but Obama's cultural liberalism, the decline of manufacturing, and the economic anxieties of the post-financial crisis era accelerated the exodus. Trump's 2016 victory in the Rust Belt states that Democrats had counted as their blue wall was the culmination of this decades-long trend.

The murder of George Floyd by Minneapolis police officer Derek Chauvin in May 2020, which was captured on video and spread rapidly across social media, triggered the largest wave of racial justice protests in American history. Biden condemned Floyd's murder and called for police reform, while Trump characterized the protests as dangerous anarchism and sent federal law enforcement to crack down on demonstrators. The racial reckoning that followed Floyd's death became a major issue in the 2020 election and continued to roil American politics in the years that followed. Republicans argued that Democratic calls for police reform were soft on crime, while Democrats argued that Republican opposition to reform was a defense of systemic racism.

The presidency of Joe Biden was defined in large part by his attempt to be the anti-Trump, to restore normalcy, competence, and decency to the White House after four years of chaos and controversy. Biden generally succeeded in restoring a more traditional style of presidential communication and in rebuilding relationships with international allies. But he was unable to overcome the structural factors driving polarization, inflation, and public dissatisfaction with the direction of the country. His decision to withdraw from the 2024 race, while praised by many as selfless, also represented an acknowledgment that his attempt to transcend the polarization of the Trump era had not fully succeeded.

Kamala Harris's historic run for president in 2024 as the first woman of color nominated by a major party ended in defeat, a result that devastated many Democrats who had hoped her candidacy would make history in a different way. Harris ran a competent and energetic campaign in the limited time available to her after Biden's withdrawal, but she was unable to overcome economic anxiety about inflation, concerns about immigration, and the weight of the Biden administration's record. Her defeat raised questions about the role of gender and race in American presidential politics, with her supporters arguing that sexism and racism had played a role in her loss and her critics arguing she had simply been a flawed candidate running in a difficult environment.

The story of American presidential politics from 2000 to the present is ultimately a story about a country trying to figure out what it wants to be. The demographic changes that are making America more diverse, the economic changes that are disrupting traditional industries and communities, the cultural changes that are transforming social norms and values, and the technological changes that are transforming how people communicate and receive information are all driving the political polarization and conflict that has defined this era. The presidency is both a product of these forces and a key arena in which they are contested, and the elections of 2000, 2004, 2008, 2012, 2016, 2020, and 2024 were all fundamentally about which vision of America would prevail.

The year 2000 election showed that America was deeply divided. The year 2008 election showed that America could embrace dramatic change. The year 2016 election showed that the change of 2008 had provoked a fierce reaction. The year 2020 election showed that even amid a pandemic and economic crisis, a majority of Americans wanted to return to something more normal. The year 2024 election showed that Trump's vision of America, with its emphasis on strength, nationalism, and rejection of liberal cultural values, maintained a durable majority in the Electoral College. The contest between these visions will continue, and the presidency will remain the highest-stakes battleground in American democracy.

Understanding the modern American presidency requires understanding the environment in which it operates. The president must manage a vast federal bureaucracy employing millions of workers. The president must navigate a Congress that is increasingly polarized and uncooperative. The president must communicate with a fragmented media landscape in which there is no longer a shared set of facts. The president must manage America's relationships with dozens of allies and adversaries around the world. And the president must do all of this while running a permanent campaign to maintain public support and prepare for the next election. The demands on the modern presidency are extraordinary, and it is perhaps not surprising that all modern presidents have fallen short in some significant ways.

George W. Bush's decision to invade Iraq in 2003 without United Nations authorization and based on faulty intelligence about weapons of mass destruction was one of the most consequential and controversial decisions in American presidential history. The invasion quickly toppled Saddam Hussein's regime, but the occupation of Iraq proved far more difficult. The disbanding of the Iraqi army contributed to the insurgency that killed thousands of American soldiers and hundreds of thousands of Iraqis. The Abu Ghraib prisoner abuse scandal, in which American soldiers were photographed humiliating and torturing Iraqi detainees, damaged America's moral credibility around the world and strengthened the insurgency.

The surge strategy that Bush implemented in early 2007, against the advice of many military commanders and Democratic opposition, involved sending 30,000 additional troops to Iraq and changing tactics to focus on protecting the civilian population. General David Petraeus, who commanded the surge, oversaw a significant reduction in sectarian violence and brought some semblance of stability to the country. Republicans pointed to the surge as a vindication of Bush's commitment to the war, while Democrats argued that the political reconciliation that the surge was supposed to enable never happened. The Iraq War ultimately cost more than 4,400 American lives and over a trillion dollars, with lasting consequences for American foreign policy and presidential credibility.

Barack Obama's foreign policy was shaped by his opposition to the Iraq War and his commitment to ending American military involvement in the region. He pursued a strategy that preferred multilateral approaches and limited direct American military involvement. Obama won the Nobel Peace Prize in 2009, before he had accomplished much in office, a decision that was widely mocked by Republicans and even acknowledged by Obama himself as premature. The Nobel Prize reflected international enthusiasm for Obama's election and his rhetorical commitment to nuclear disarmament and multilateral diplomacy.

Obama's signature foreign policy achievement was the Iran nuclear deal, formally known as the Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action, which was negotiated in 2015 and placed significant restrictions on Iran's nuclear program in exchange for the lifting of international sanctions. Obama argued the deal was the best available option for preventing Iran from developing a nuclear weapon without going to war. Republicans argued it was a catastrophically bad agreement that gave Iran billions of dollars and allowed its nuclear program to continue. Trump withdrew from the Iran deal in 2018, calling it the worst deal ever made, and reimposed sanctions on Iran.

The relationship between American presidents and Congress was fundamentally reshaped by the polarization of this era. Bipartisan cooperation, once common on issues ranging from national security to domestic policy, became increasingly rare. The era of presidents working with members of the opposing party to pass major legislation seemed to have ended. The few examples of bipartisan legislation in this era, like the 9/11 authorization for use of force, the TARP bailout, and Biden's infrastructure bill, stood out precisely because they were so unusual. The Senate filibuster ensured that partisan majorities alone were rarely sufficient to pass significant legislation.

The Republican Party's relationship with business and economic elites evolved significantly during the Trump era. Republicans had long been seen as the party of business, supporting lower taxes, less regulation, and free trade. Trump's tariffs and his attacks on specific companies that he felt had slighted him or the American worker represented a significant departure from this tradition. Some corporate leaders who had supported previous Republican presidents distanced themselves from Trump, particularly after January 6. But the Trump tax cut of 2017, which cut the corporate tax rate from 35 percent to 21 percent, was enormously popular with corporate America, and most business leaders maintained their political support for the Republican agenda even when they had reservations about Trump personally.

The Democratic Party's relationship with business also evolved during this period. Obama had worked closely with Wall Street in responding to the financial crisis, and his administration was staffed with many financial industry veterans. Democrats like Warren and Sanders argued that this cozy relationship with Wall Street had corrupted the party's economic policy and contributed to the economic anxiety that drove the 2016 revolt. Biden came into office promising to be more aggressive in regulating large corporations and more supportive of labor unions than previous Democratic presidents. His appointments of antitrust regulators represented a genuine shift in Democratic economic policy.

The Supreme Court appointments of this era had profound consequences for American law and society that went well beyond the abortion issue. The conservative majority that solidified under Trump's three appointments expanded gun rights, limited the ability of federal agencies to regulate, weakened campaign finance restrictions, and made it harder to sue corporations for civil rights violations. The Court also ruled in favor of religious liberty claims in ways that Democrats argued allowed discrimination against LGBTQ individuals. The transformation of the Supreme Court was one of the most lasting consequences of Trump's presidency and one that continued to shape American life long after he left and returned to office.

The role of vice president changed and grew during this period. Dick Cheney was arguably the most powerful vice president in American history, exercising enormous influence over Bush's national security and foreign policy. Joe Biden was a more traditional vice president, serving as Obama's chief legislative liaison and loyal partner. Mike Pence served as Trump's loyal deputy for nearly all of the Trump presidency, only to break with Trump on January 6 when he refused to go along with Trump's demand that he reject the certified electoral votes of battleground states. Pence's refusal to comply with Trump's demand, which legal scholars agreed was unconstitutional, made him a hero to some and a traitor to others in the Republican Party. Kamala Harris served as Biden's vice president and first Black and South Asian woman in that role before becoming the Democratic presidential nominee.

The politics of taxes has been a fundamental dividing line between the parties throughout this period. Republicans have consistently supported tax cuts, arguing that lower taxes stimulate economic growth, reduce government waste, and allow individuals to keep more of what they earn. Democrats have argued that Republican tax cuts primarily benefit the wealthy and corporations while adding to the deficit and shortchanging investments in education, infrastructure, and healthcare. Bush cut taxes twice, primarily benefiting upper income Americans. Obama let many of the Bush tax cuts expire for higher income earners. Trump dramatically cut corporate taxes and provided significant benefits to wealthy individuals. Biden proposed raising taxes on corporations and high earners to fund his social programs but was largely blocked by Senate Democrats and Republicans.

The politics of energy and climate change divided the parties in ways that had lasting consequences. Republicans generally supported expanding fossil fuel production and opposed government mandates to transition to clean energy, arguing that market forces should determine the energy mix and that climate regulations would harm economic growth and energy security. Democrats generally supported transitioning to clean energy to address climate change, arguing that the costs of inaction were far greater than the costs of the transition. Bush opened more federal lands to oil and gas drilling. Obama promoted natural gas as a bridge fuel while supporting clean energy investment. Trump withdrew from the Paris Climate Agreement and aggressively expanded fossil fuel production on federal lands. Biden rejoined Paris, invested hundreds of billions in clean energy, but also approved significant oil and gas projects to manage energy prices.

The economy as an issue in presidential politics was transformed by the financial crisis of 2008. The crash and the subsequent slow recovery created lasting anxieties about economic security and inequality that politicians of both parties tried to address. Obama presided over a recovery that was long and steady but felt inadequate to many Americans. Trump won partly by promising faster growth and better deals for American workers. Trump's pre-pandemic economy was strong, with low unemployment and rising wages, but the pandemic wiped out millions of jobs. Biden inherited a recovering economy but presided over the highest inflation in forty years, which proved to be one of the most significant political liabilities any modern president had faced.

The story of the modern American presidency is also the story of media transformation. When Bush took office, the major broadcast networks and a handful of newspapers largely set the news agenda. Fox News was new and growing but had not yet achieved total dominance of conservative media. By the end of this period, the media landscape had been transformed beyond recognition. Social media platforms had become the primary news source for millions of Americans. Conservative media had fragmented into dozens of websites, podcasts, and streaming channels that competed to out-Trump Trump. Liberal media had similarly fragmented, with numerous digital outlets and podcasts competing for the attention of Democratic voters. The fragmentation of media made it harder for any president to command the kind of national attention that presidents once could, except through controversy and conflict.

The American presidency has always been the most powerful office in the world, and the decisions made by American presidents in the 21st century affected not just American citizens but people around the globe. The decision to invade Iraq created instability that contributed to the rise of the Islamic State and a refugee crisis that destabilized Europe. Obama's approach to the Arab Spring contributed to the chaos that followed the overthrow of authoritarian governments in Libya, Egypt, and elsewhere. Trump's trade war with China escalated tensions between the world's two largest economies and disrupted global supply chains. Biden's management of the Ukraine war and his efforts to build a coalition against Russian aggression tested the resilience of the post-World War Two international order.

The modern American president must navigate a world of 24-hour news cycles, social media controversy, partisan polarization, and global interdependence that would be unrecognizable to the founders who designed the office. The constitutional structure of American government, with its separation of powers and system of checks and balances, was designed to prevent tyranny but also to make governance difficult. The polarization of the modern era has made governance even more difficult, with presidents often unable to work with Congress and resorting to executive actions that their successors promptly reverse. Whether the American constitutional system can adapt to these pressures while maintaining its essential democratic character is the central question facing American democracy in the years ahead.

The legacy of the Bush, Obama, Trump, and Biden presidencies will be debated by historians for decades. Each president faced extraordinary challenges and made decisions that will shape the country for generations. Each was the product of and a contributor to the polarization that has divided the country. Each tried in his own way to be the president not just of his party but of all Americans, and each fell short of that aspiration in some significant ways. The American presidency, for all its power and all its failures, remains the central institution of American democracy, the office that most directly embodies the aspirations and anxieties of the American people, and the office through which the great contests over America's future are ultimately decided.

The Republican Party under Trump won presidential elections by assembling a coalition of white working-class voters, evangelical Christians, rural Americans, and small business owners who felt left behind by the globalization, cultural change, and elite consensus that had defined American life for decades. Trump gave voice to their grievances in a direct and often crude way that felt authentic to his supporters even when it violated every norm of presidential behavior. His followers believed he was fighting for them against a corrupt political establishment that had ignored their interests for decades. This bond between Trump and his base proved extraordinarily durable, surviving scandals, impeachments, criminal indictments, and an attack on the Capitol that would have destroyed any other political career.

The Democratic Party under Obama and Biden won presidential elections by assembling a coalition of African Americans, Latinos, Asian Americans, college-educated whites, young voters, and urban professionals. This coalition reflected the demographic changes transforming America and the cultural values of a diverse, educated, urban population. Democrats argued their coalition was the future of America, that demographic change was inevitable and would eventually produce a durable Democratic majority. This prediction seemed plausible after Obama's victories but was complicated by Trump's ability to win Rust Belt states in 2016 and to make gains with Latino and working-class voters of color in 2020 and 2024.

The geographic distribution of American voters has created a structural advantage for Republicans in the Electoral College that Democrats have struggled to overcome. Because Democratic voters are concentrated in large cities on the coasts, and because the Electoral College gives equal weight to votes regardless of state population, a smaller number of Republican voters spread across a larger number of states can sometimes defeat a larger number of Democratic voters concentrated in fewer places. This dynamic produced the controversial results of 2000 and 2016, when the popular vote winner lost the Electoral College. Democrats have called for abolishing the Electoral College, while Republicans have defended it as a protection for rural and small-state interests.

The history of the modern American presidency is also a history of expanding and contracting presidential power. Bush expanded presidential power dramatically in the aftermath of September 11, asserting a unilateral right to conduct surveillance, authorize torture, and detain individuals indefinitely without trial. Obama initially promised to roll back these expansions but largely maintained them while adding the controversial practice of targeted drone killings of American citizens abroad. Trump pushed presidential power in new directions, using the pardon power for political allies, asserting executive privilege to shield his administration from congressional oversight, and claiming that presidents could not be investigated or indicted while in office. Biden took a more restrained approach but continued to rely on executive action when Congress was unable to pass legislation.

The question of whether any modern president can truly unite a divided country has become increasingly doubtful. Obama was elected on a platform of hope and change and a promise to transcend the red state and blue state divide that had defined the country under Bush. His presidency united the country briefly in the aftermath of events like the killing of Osama bin Laden, but otherwise the polarization deepened throughout his tenure. Trump made no pretense of trying to unite the country, explicitly governing for his base and treating his opponents as enemies. Biden promised unity but found that the structural forces driving division were too powerful for any individual president to overcome. The presidency that once served as a unifying symbol of American nationhood has become one of the most divisive institutions in American life.

The relationship between American presidents and the military has been a complex one in this era. Bush launched two wars and had to manage the relationship with military commanders who sometimes chafed at his decisions. Obama's relationship with the military was complicated by his decisions to scale back American military involvement abroad and his use of drone strikes, which the military sometimes favored over more traditional forms of combat. Trump's relationship with the military was tumultuous, with multiple former generals and top military officials eventually speaking out against him and characterizing him as a threat to democratic institutions. Biden returned to a more traditional civil-military relationship, though his decision on Afghanistan withdrawal was controversial within the military.

The story of American presidential politics in the first quarter of the 21st century is ultimately a story about the fragility and resilience of democracy. The democratic institutions of the United States survived the contested election of 2000, the trauma of September 11, the financial crisis of 2008, the pandemic of 2020, and the January 6 attack on the Capitol. Each of these events tested those institutions in different ways, and each time they ultimately held. But each test left marks, and the cumulative effect of two decades of polarization, norm-breaking, and declining trust in democratic institutions has left American democracy more fragile than it was when the century began.

The American people have repeatedly demonstrated their capacity for self-governance, their willingness to hold elections under difficult circumstances, and their ultimate commitment to the peaceful transfer of power even when they disagree bitterly about who should hold power. The elections of the 21st century have been contested, litigated, and sometimes accompanied by violence, but the results have ultimately been accepted and implemented. The presidency has changed hands between the parties multiple times, demonstrating that no single party or faction can permanently capture the state and bend it to its exclusive purposes. These are the strengths of American democracy that must be preserved and defended against the forces of polarization and authoritarian temptation that have grown stronger in recent years.

The story continues with Trump's return to the White House in January 2025, beginning an unprecedented second non-consecutive term that raised profound questions about the future of American democracy, the rule of law, and the international order that the United States had helped build and sustain for eighty years. Whether this chapter would end in the further entrenchment of Trumpism, a restoration of something closer to the pre-Trump normal, or some new configuration of American politics that could not yet be predicted remained to be seen. What was certain was that the American presidency would remain at the center of this story, the office through which the country's deepest conflicts were expressed and through which its most consequential choices were made.

Trump's return to the White House in 2025 was accompanied by a sweeping program of executive action that sought to transform the federal government and reverse the policies of the Biden years. Trump signed dozens of executive orders on his first day in office, targeting immigration enforcement, diversity programs in the federal government, climate regulations, and international commitments. The speed and scope of the executive actions were unprecedented and reflected the determination of Trump and his allies to learn from the first term and move more decisively to implement the agenda that his supporters had elected him to pursue. Democrats challenged many of the actions in court, beginning a new cycle of legal battles over the scope of presidential power.

The Republican coalition that Trump had assembled continued to hold together in his second term, united by shared grievances against the political, cultural, and media establishment that Republicans blamed for the problems of the country. Trump's supporters believed he was fighting a corrupt system on their behalf, and this belief made them remarkably tolerant of his behavior, his rhetoric, and his legal troubles. The Republican Party had become in many ways a personal vehicle for Trump rather than a traditional political party with its own independent identity and agenda. How long this arrangement could last, and what would happen to the Republican Party after Trump eventually left the political stage, were questions that would define the future of American politics.

The Democratic Party was in a period of reflection and rebuilding after its 2024 defeat. The party faced difficult questions about its coalition, its messaging, and its candidates. The progressive wing argued that the party had lost by failing to offer a bold enough vision of economic change that could win back working-class voters. The moderate wing argued that the party had lost by moving too far left on cultural issues that alienated persuadable voters. Both sides agreed that the party needed new leadership and new ideas but disagreed sharply about what those should be. The search for the next generation of Democratic leaders was underway, with governors, senators, and other officials positioning themselves as the future of a party that was trying to find its footing in the post-Biden era.

The interplay between the two parties, between Trump and his opponents, between the progressive and moderate wings of the Democratic Party, and between competing visions of American identity and direction will continue to shape American presidential politics for years to come. The office of the presidency will remain the central prize in this contest, the office that most directly determines the direction of the country and the fate of American democracy. Understanding the history of that office in the 21st century, the decisions that were made and the forces that shaped them, is essential to understanding the country that America has become and the choices that it faces in the years ahead.

The Republican Party first won the White House in 2000 with a candidate who had lost the popular vote and won only through a Supreme Court decision that halted a recount in Florida. This inauspicious beginning set the tone for a quarter century of Republican presidential politics that would be defined by norm-breaking, polarization, and an increasingly tenuous connection between the party's electoral base and the majority of the American people. Trump took this to its ultimate conclusion, winning the presidency twice while losing the popular vote once and barely winning it a second time, building a political movement that was explicitly hostile to the liberal cultural values of the majority of Americans but that proved capable of winning the Electoral College through its geographic concentration in rural and small-town America.

The Democratic Party won three of the five presidential popular votes in the 21st century's first quarter but held the presidency for only twelve of the first 25 years, a reflection of the structural disadvantages the party faces in the Electoral College. Obama's two victories demonstrated that a Democratic candidate with exceptional political skills and a powerful coalition could win comfortable Electoral College majorities. Clinton's 2016 defeat demonstrated that a Democratic candidate who was unable to generate enthusiasm among the base and who failed to campaign effectively in the Rust Belt could lose the Electoral College despite winning the popular vote. Biden's 2020 victory demonstrated that a reassuring moderate could beat Trump but that the margins were narrow. Harris's 2024 defeat demonstrated that the structural challenges facing Democrats in the Electoral College had not been solved.

The story of the American presidency in the 21st century is a story of extraordinary events, consequential decisions, and fundamental conflicts over the nature of American democracy. It is a story that is still being written, with outcomes uncertain and the future of American self-governance genuinely in doubt in ways that would have seemed unimaginable at the start of the century. The institutions of American democracy have proven more resilient than many feared, but they have also proven more fragile than many assumed. The choices made by American voters, politicians, and institutions in the years ahead will determine whether the 21st century is ultimately a story of democratic renewal or democratic decline. The American presidency, for all its power and all its problems, will remain at the center of that story.

The election of 2000 between George W. Bush and Al Gore remains one of the most disputed and consequential contests in the history of American democracy. Bush, the Republican governor of Texas and son of a former president, faced off against Gore, the sitting Democratic vice president who had served eight years alongside Bill Clinton. The race was extraordinarily close, and on election night the television networks first called Florida for Gore, then reversed their call and gave it to Bush, and then declared the state too close to call. The final outcome hinged on a few hundred votes in Florida, and the legal battle that followed dragged on for 36 days before the Supreme Court issued its ruling in Bush v. Gore, halting the recount by a five to four margin. Gore, who had received more votes from Americans nationwide than Bush, accepted the outcome and urged the country to move forward, in what many historians have called one of the most gracious concession speeches in American political history.

Bush entered the White House as a deeply divisive figure, seen by Democrats as having stolen the election and by Republicans as having fought for and won a legitimate victory. His early presidency focused on his domestic agenda, including a massive tax cut that Congress passed in the spring of 2001. But everything changed when al-Qaeda terrorists attacked the United States on September 11, 2001, using four hijacked commercial aircraft as weapons. The attacks killed nearly 3,000 people and transformed Bush from a relatively unremarkable domestic policy president into a wartime commander-in-chief who would define the next decade of American foreign policy. Bush's response, which included the invasion of Afghanistan in October 2001 and the more controversial invasion of Iraq in March 2003, set America on a path of military engagement that would outlast his presidency by many years.

Bush's decision to invade Iraq was based on the assertion that Saddam Hussein possessed weapons of mass destruction and that removing him from power was necessary for American national security. This assertion turned out to be false, and no weapons of mass destruction were found after the invasion. The intelligence failure was catastrophic, but Bush pressed forward with the occupation, insisting that the removal of Saddam and the establishment of a democratic Iraq were worth the cost. Democrats who had initially voted to authorize the war, including Biden, Kerry, and Clinton, later faced political pressure to explain and defend those votes. The Iraq War became a defining issue of American politics for over a decade, contributing to the election of Obama, who had opposed it, and complicating the campaigns of Democrats who had supported it.

The financial crisis that erupted in 2008 was the product of years of reckless lending in the housing market, inadequate regulation of complex financial instruments, and systemic failures at major financial institutions. When the bubble burst, the consequences were devastating. Lehman Brothers, one of the oldest and largest investment banks in the United States, filed for bankruptcy in September 2008, triggering a global financial panic. The federal government was forced to intervene on an unprecedented scale, with the Treasury Department and the Federal Reserve providing trillions of dollars in loans, guarantees, and direct investments to prevent the complete collapse of the financial system. Bush signed the Troubled Asset Relief Program into law despite his free-market instincts, arguing that the situation required extraordinary measures.

The financial crisis destroyed Bush's remaining political capital and created the conditions for Obama's historic victory. Voters blamed Republicans for the economic catastrophe, and Bush left office with an approval rating in the low twenties, one of the worst of any departing president in the polling era. His vice president, Dick Cheney, was even more unpopular, seen by many Americans as the real power behind the Bush throne and the architect of the most controversial policies of the administration. Cheney's influence on the Bush administration was extraordinary and largely unprecedented in American history, with the vice president playing a central role in shaping national security policy, energy policy, and the administration's approach to presidential power.

Obama's election in 2008 was celebrated around the world as a historic breakthrough for racial equality and a repudiation of the Bush era. Obama was a brilliant orator and a skilled political operator who had risen from relative obscurity to the presidency in just four years. His campaign was a masterclass in modern political organizing, using the internet and social media in ways that had never been done before to mobilize volunteers and raise money from millions of small donors. Obama's message of hope and change resonated with a country exhausted by war, shaken by financial crisis, and hungry for something new. His victory margin in the popular vote and the Electoral College was decisive, and Democrats swept to large majorities in Congress, creating the possibility of transformative legislation.

Obama's first term was dominated by the twin challenges of stabilizing the economy and passing healthcare reform. The stimulus package he signed in February 2009 was the largest single piece of fiscal legislation in American history, combining tax cuts, infrastructure spending, and aid to states to prevent the recession from deepening into a depression. Economists disagree about how much the stimulus helped, but the economy did stabilize and then begin a slow recovery that would continue for the rest of Obama's presidency. The recovery was slower and more unequal than Obama had hoped, with corporate profits and stock prices recovering quickly while wages stagnated and many working-class communities continued to struggle.

The Affordable Care Act was the most ambitious and consequential piece of domestic legislation since Lyndon Johnson's Great Society programs. The law required all Americans to obtain health insurance, either through their employer, through expanded Medicaid, or through new insurance marketplaces where subsidies were available for those with low incomes. Insurance companies were required to cover people with pre-existing conditions and could not charge women more than men for the same coverage. Young adults could stay on their parents' insurance until age 26. The law was imperfect and a compromise from what many Democrats had originally wanted, but it extended coverage to tens of millions of previously uninsured Americans and fundamentally changed the healthcare landscape.

Republicans hated the ACA with an intensity that surprised even the law's critics. They filed multiple court challenges, made dozens of repeal votes in Congress, and made repealing Obamacare a central plank of their electoral platform for over a decade. The law survived a Supreme Court challenge in 2012 when Chief Justice John Roberts, appointed by Bush, cast the deciding vote to uphold it on the grounds that the individual mandate was a valid exercise of Congress's taxing power. This decision infuriated conservative Republicans and began a process of alienation from Roberts that would continue for years. The ACA also survived repeal attempts in Congress in 2017, when Republican efforts to pass alternative legislation collapsed due to the opposition of several Republican senators, including the late John McCain, whose dramatic thumbs-down vote against the skinny repeal bill became one of the most memorable moments of the Trump era.

Obama's reelection in 2012 against Mitt Romney was a decisive validation of his first term and a rejection of the Romney vision of dramatically smaller government and lower taxes on the wealthy. Romney was a capable politician and an intelligent man who had real accomplishments as a governor and businessman, but he struggled to connect with ordinary voters and made several high-profile mistakes, including his secretly recorded comment about the 47 percent of Americans who paid no income taxes and were dependent on government. Obama won by mobilizing his coalition of young voters, African Americans, Latinos, and college-educated women, while Romney carried white voters without college degrees by a substantial margin. The demographic divide that would define the next decade of American politics was already clearly visible in the 2012 results.

Obama's second term was complicated by a series of foreign policy crises that challenged his preference for diplomacy over military force. The Syrian civil war, which began in 2011, drew in multiple regional and global powers and produced one of the worst humanitarian catastrophes of the 21st century. Obama drew a red line against the use of chemical weapons by Syrian President Bashar al-Assad but then declined to enforce it militarily when Assad crossed the line, a decision that Republicans argued had damaged American credibility around the world. The rise of the Islamic State, also known as ISIS or ISIL, in 2014 forced Obama to recommit American military forces to the region, with a combination of air strikes and special operations forces that limited American casualties but also limited American effectiveness.

Russia's annexation of Crimea from Ukraine in 2014 was the most serious European security crisis in decades. Obama responded with economic sanctions and diplomatic pressure but declined to provide Ukraine with lethal military assistance, arguing that the risks of escalation were too great. Republicans accused Obama of weakness and appeasement, drawing comparisons to the failures of pre-World War Two diplomacy. Democrats argued that Obama's measured response prevented a potentially catastrophic military escalation. The Ukraine crisis foreshadowed the much larger Russian invasion that would come under Biden in 2022, and the debates about how to respond to Russian aggression that had begun under Obama would continue through the Biden presidency and into the Trump second term.

Trump's entry into the presidential race in 2015 was initially treated as a joke by the political establishment, including most Republicans. The conventional wisdom was that his inflammatory rhetoric, his lack of government experience, and his history of business failures and scandals would disqualify him. This conventional wisdom turned out to be completely wrong. Trump understood something that the political establishment had missed: a significant portion of the Republican base was furious at the party's establishment, at free trade agreements that had cost American manufacturing jobs, at immigration that had changed the cultural character of many communities, and at a political class that talked about the concerns of ordinary Americans but never seemed to do anything about them. Trump gave voice to this fury in a direct and often crude way that felt authentic to his supporters even when it was factually inaccurate or morally objectionable.

Trump's 2016 victory was built on a geographic realignment that had been building for years but that surprised almost everyone when it happened. By winning Pennsylvania, Michigan, and Wisconsin, states that had voted Democratic in every presidential election since 1988, Trump demonstrated that the Rust Belt was no longer reliably Democratic. These states had lost millions of manufacturing jobs in the decades since NAFTA was implemented, and Trump's attacks on free trade and his promises to bring manufacturing back resonated with working-class voters who had seen their communities hollowed out. Democrats had taken these voters for granted for too long, and Trump paid attention to them at precisely the moment when they were most receptive to his message.

Trump's presidency was the most disruptive in modern American history. He fired more senior officials, including his own appointments, than any previous president. He attacked the press, the judiciary, the intelligence community, and his own party's leadership when they crossed him or failed to sufficiently support him. He made policy by tweet, announcing major decisions without consulting his own advisors. He withdrew from the Trans-Pacific Partnership on his first full day in office, then withdrew from the Paris Climate Agreement, then withdrew from the Iran nuclear deal, then withdrew from the intermediate-range nuclear forces treaty with Russia. His foreign policy was characterized by hostility to traditional allies and unexpected friendliness toward authoritarian leaders like Vladimir Putin and Kim Jong-un. Democrats condemned every aspect of his presidency; Republicans largely celebrated it.

The Mueller investigation hung over Trump's presidency for two years, and the report's release in April 2019 was one of the most anticipated political events of the era. The report documented in extraordinary detail the extent of Russian interference in the 2016 election, including the hacking of Democratic emails, the social media manipulation campaign, and multiple contacts between Trump campaign officials and Russian operatives. It also documented more than ten instances of potential obstruction of justice by Trump. But Mueller concluded he could not make a traditional prosecutorial judgment about the president's guilt due to Justice Department policy against indicting a sitting president, and he left the question of obstruction to Congress. Democrats called for impeachment; Republicans called it a witch hunt.

Trump's first impeachment in December 2019 centered on his actions toward Ukraine, specifically his decision to withhold congressionally appropriated military assistance to Ukraine in order to pressure the Ukrainian government to announce an investigation into Joe Biden and his son Hunter Biden. The whistleblower complaint that triggered the impeachment inquiry revealed the details of a phone call between Trump and Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelensky in which Trump appeared to condition the release of military aid on the announcement of investigations. Democrats argued this was a clear abuse of power, a corrupt use of American foreign policy for personal political advantage. Republicans argued the phone call was perfect and that Democrats were engaged in a partisan coup attempt.

The Senate acquittal of Trump in February 2020 was almost entirely along party lines, with only Mitt Romney crossing over to vote to convict on the abuse of power charge, making him the first senator in American history to vote to convict a president of his own party. Romney's vote was a display of moral courage that made him a hero to Democrats and a traitor to Republicans. Trump never forgave Romney and spent years trying to undermine him. Romney eventually decided not to seek reelection to the Senate, citing the toxic political environment and the futility of trying to maintain principled conservatism in a party that had been completely captured by Trump.

The COVID-19 pandemic transformed every aspect of American life and politics in 2020. The virus first appeared in China in late 2019 and reached the United States in early 2020, spreading rapidly through nursing homes, urban centers, and eventually every part of the country. Trump's initial response was characterized by denial and minimization, with Trump repeatedly predicting that the virus would disappear and compare it to the seasonal flu. When the pandemic became impossible to minimize, Trump shifted to attacking China for releasing the virus and attacking state governors, mostly Democrats, who imposed the public health restrictions that health experts said were necessary to slow the spread. The federal government's response was chaotic and inadequate, with shortages of personal protective equipment, testing supplies, and ventilators creating crises in the hardest-hit states.

Biden's campaign in 2020 was largely conducted virtually due to the pandemic, with Biden holding small socially distanced events rather than the large rallies that Trump continued to hold despite public health concerns. Biden's message was simple: the country was in crisis because of Trump's failures, and Biden would restore competence and decency to the White House. He promised to follow the science on COVID-19, to repair America's relationships with its allies, to address the economic inequality that the pandemic had exposed, and to restore the norms of democratic governance that Trump had violated. The message was appealing to the college-educated suburban voters who had moved toward Democrats during the Trump years, and it held together enough of the Obama coalition to produce a comfortable Electoral College victory.

Biden's victory was marred by Trump's refusal to accept the results. Beginning on election night, when Trump falsely declared victory and accused Democrats of stealing the election, Trump launched the most sustained and dangerous assault on American democratic norms since the Civil War. His legal team filed over 60 lawsuits challenging the results in battleground states, all of which were rejected by courts including those with Trump-appointed judges. His attorney general, William Barr, publicly stated that the Justice Department had found no evidence of widespread fraud sufficient to change the outcome. Republican election officials in Georgia, Arizona, and other states certified Biden's victory and resisted enormous pressure from Trump and his allies to reject or change the certified results.

The January 6 attack on the Capitol was the direct result of Trump's months-long campaign to convince his supporters that the election had been stolen. Thousands of Trump supporters gathered in Washington on the day Congress was scheduled to certify the electoral college results, responding to Trump's invitation for a wild time. After Trump addressed the crowd, urging them to march to the Capitol and fight like hell, thousands of them did exactly that. The mob overpowered Capitol Police, broke windows, ransacked offices, and occupied the Senate floor while lawmakers sheltered in secured locations. Vice President Mike Pence, who had refused Trump's demand to reject the certified electoral votes, was evacuated as rioters chanted hang Mike Pence. The attack delayed but did not stop the certification of Biden's victory, which was completed in the early hours of January 7.

Biden's first year in office was marked by significant legislative accomplishments and significant setbacks. The American Rescue Plan provided direct relief to millions of Americans suffering from the pandemic's economic effects. The infrastructure law was the most significant investment in American physical infrastructure in decades. But Biden's broader social spending agenda, embodied in the Build Back Better legislation, collapsed when Democratic senators Manchin and Sinema refused to support it in its full form. The failure to pass Build Back Better disappointed the progressive wing of the party and raised questions about Biden's ability to deliver on his promises to be a transformative president.

Biden's foreign policy was more conventionally internationalist than Trump's, with Biden making the rebuilding of alliances and the reassertion of American global leadership a central priority. He rejoined the Paris Climate Agreement and the World Health Organization on his first day. He hosted the Summit for Democracy to signal America's recommitment to democratic values. He organized the most extensive NATO response to Russian aggression since the Cold War when Putin invaded Ukraine in February 2022. The Ukraine response was genuinely impressive, with Biden and his team coordinating an unprecedented package of economic sanctions and military assistance that prevented Russia from achieving a quick victory. But the war ground on, and as it extended into years, Republican support began to erode.

The 2022 midterm elections were a disappointment for Republicans and a relief for Democrats. The expected red wave that would give Republicans large majorities in both chambers failed to materialize. Democrats lost the House but by a narrow margin, and they gained a seat in the Senate. The relatively strong Democratic performance was attributed to the Supreme Court's decision to overturn Roe v. Wade, which mobilized Democratic voters who had been dispirited by Biden's low approval ratings and the challenges of his first two years. The abortion issue proved to be a powerful motivator for Democratic base voters and for suburban women who had moved toward Republicans during the Trump years but came back to Democrats when reproductive rights were under direct threat.

The 2024 Republican primary was one of the strangest in American political history. Trump was the dominant frontrunner from the moment he announced in November 2022, and his rivals struggled to figure out how to challenge him without alienating the Republican voters who remained devoted to him. Ron DeSantis was widely seen as the most viable alternative, having won a massive reelection victory in Florida and built a national reputation as a no-nonsense conservative who could deliver results without the chaos and controversy of Trump. But DeSantis's campaign was troubled from the start, marked by an awkward launch, an inability to connect with voters on a personal level, and multiple strategic missteps. He suspended his campaign before the Iowa caucuses.

Nikki Haley was Trump's most persistent rival, staying in the race through the early states and winning significant support from college-educated Republicans who were looking for an alternative. Her background as governor of South Carolina and ambassador to the United Nations gave her credibility on both domestic and foreign policy, and her crisp debating style contrasted favorably with Trump's rambling and evasive performances. But Haley could not break through Trump's dominance among the committed Republican base, and she eventually suspended her campaign in early March 2024. Her decision to withhold her endorsement of Trump for several weeks was seen as a calculated move to position herself for future races.

The general election between Trump and Harris was one of the most unprecedented in American history. Harris had only three and a half months to introduce herself to voters as a presidential candidate, having been overshadowed in her role as vice president and having been defined largely by Republicans as an incompetent leftist. She ran an energetic and disciplined campaign that generated genuine enthusiasm from the Democratic base, particularly among young voters and voters of color. But she struggled to convince the persuadable voters in the battleground states who were unhappy with inflation and immigration under the Biden administration that she would do things differently while still defending the administration's record. Trump's message of economic restoration and immigration restriction ultimately proved more compelling to the crucial swing voters in Pennsylvania, Michigan, Wisconsin, Georgia, Arizona, and Nevada.

The way that Americans have chosen their presidents in the 21st century reflects the deep divisions and competing visions that characterize contemporary American political life. Republicans have consistently rallied around candidates who promise to reduce the size and role of government, protect traditional values, and assert American strength abroad. Democrats have consistently rallied around candidates who promise to use government to address inequality, protect civil rights, and engage constructively with international partners. These competing visions have produced elections that are close, contested, and consequential, with the outcomes determining not just who occupies the White House but the direction of the country's policies for years or decades.

George W. Bush's compassionate conservatism was his attempt to update the Reagan Republican formula for the 21st century, combining traditional Republican tax cuts and deregulation with a more activist approach to social programs like education and faith-based initiatives. Bush genuinely believed that government could play a constructive role in addressing poverty and inequality, and he was a strong advocate for increased foreign aid and AIDS relief in Africa. His No Child Left Behind Act was a bipartisan effort to improve educational outcomes through increased testing and accountability that received support from Democratic senator Ted Kennedy. But these moderate impulses were overwhelmed by the Iraq War and its consequences, which defined his presidency and consumed his political capital.

Dick Cheney's influence on the Bush presidency deserves special attention because it was so unusual and so consequential. As a former secretary of defense, former White House chief of staff, and former congressman, Cheney had more experience in government than Bush and exercised more influence over major policy decisions than any previous vice president. He was a driving force behind the decision to invade Iraq, the authorization of enhanced interrogation techniques, and the expansion of surveillance programs. He operated largely out of public view, preferring to exercise influence through private channels rather than public statements. His relationship with Bush was not without tension, but for most of the first term and much of the second, Cheney was able to steer administration policy in a consistently hawkish and conservative direction.

The Tea Party movement that emerged in 2009 and 2010 was the most significant grassroots political movement of the early Obama years and a harbinger of the populist revolt that would eventually produce Trump. Tea Party activists were animated by opposition to the stimulus, the ACA, and what they saw as Obama's big government agenda, but they were also motivated by cultural anxieties about changing demographics and values. The movement channeled these anxieties into electoral action, helping Republicans gain 63 seats in the House in 2010 and set the stage for years of Republican obstruction of Obama's agenda. Many Tea Party activists would later become Trump supporters, seeing in Trump a more effective and more authentic voice for their grievances than the conventional politicians who had ridden the Tea Party wave to power.

The birther movement, which Trump promoted for years before running for president, was one of the most consequential and revealing episodes of the Obama era. The birther conspiracy theory held that Obama had not been born in the United States and was therefore constitutionally ineligible to serve as president. The theory was false, definitively disproven by the release of Obama's birth certificate, and was widely recognized as motivated by racial resentment. But Trump promoted it aggressively and built a significant following among Republican voters who believed or wanted to believe that Obama was illegitimate. Trump's willingness to promote this racist conspiracy theory while the rest of the Republican establishment either ignored it or tepidly rejected it demonstrated his intuitive understanding of the racial anxieties that animated a significant portion of the Republican base.

Obama's presidency produced significant achievements on foreign policy that his critics underestimated and his supporters sometimes took for granted. The Iran nuclear deal, negotiated over years of painstaking diplomacy, placed significant restrictions on Iran's nuclear program and subjected it to unprecedented international inspection. The Trans-Pacific Partnership would have created a high-standards trade agreement with eleven Pacific Rim nations that would have served as a counterweight to China's economic influence. The Paris Climate Agreement was the first global accord in which all major economies committed to reducing their greenhouse gas emissions. All three of these agreements were dismantled by Trump in his first term, illustrating how consequential elections are for the continuity of foreign policy.

Obama's deportation record is one of the more complicated aspects of his presidency for his supporters to reconcile. Despite his reputation as a champion of immigrant rights, Obama deported more people than any previous president in history, earning him the nickname deporter in chief from some immigration advocates. Obama argued that aggressive enforcement was necessary to build Republican support for comprehensive immigration reform and to demonstrate that Democrats could be tough on enforcement. The strategy did not work, and comprehensive immigration reform remained elusive. Obama's Deferred Action for Childhood Arrivals program, which protected young undocumented immigrants who had been brought to the country as children, was a significant step in the right direction but was far short of the comprehensive reform that advocates had sought.

Trump's promise to build a wall on the southern border was one of the most memorable and consequential promises of his 2016 campaign. The wall became a symbol of his immigration agenda, promising a physical barrier that would stop the flow of undocumented immigrants and drugs across the southern border. Congress ultimately appropriated money for some barrier construction, but far less than Trump wanted, and Trump declared a national emergency to redirect other federal funds to wall construction. Democrats and many Republicans argued the emergency declaration was an unconstitutional circumvention of Congress's power of the purse. Courts blocked some of the wall funding. Biden halted construction on his first day in office, and many of the materials that had been purchased sat unused in the desert for years.

Trump's relationship with Vladimir Putin was one of the most controversial aspects of his presidency. Trump consistently refused to criticize Putin personally, even when confronted with evidence of Russian interference in American elections, Russian aggression against Ukraine, and the poisoning of Russian dissidents and opposition leaders. Trump's defenders argued that he was simply trying to maintain a productive relationship with a major nuclear power. His critics argued that Trump's deference to Putin was dangerous, inexplicable, and possibly the product of compromising information or financial entanglements that Russia held over him. The Mueller investigation found extensive Russian efforts to help Trump win the 2016 election but did not establish that Trump himself had directly conspired with Russia in this effort.

The impeachment of Trump over Ukraine in 2019 revealed the depth of the corruption of American foreign policy that had occurred during his presidency. By using congressionally appropriated military aid as leverage to extract personal political favors, Trump had fundamentally violated the principle that American foreign policy should serve the national interest rather than the personal interests of the president. The career foreign service officers and national security officials who testified against Trump during the impeachment inquiry were remarkable people who risked their careers to tell the truth about what they had witnessed. Their testimony provided a damning portrait of a president who treated American foreign policy as a tool for personal enrichment and political advancement.

The January 6 attack on the Capitol was not a spontaneous event but the culmination of a weeks-long effort by Trump and his allies to prevent the peaceful transfer of power. This effort included pressuring state officials to reverse their certified results, pressuring Vice President Pence to reject the certified electoral votes, and organizing the rally that preceded the attack on the Capitol. The House Select Committee that investigated January 6 documented this effort in extraordinary detail, revealing the extent to which Trump and his inner circle had planned and prepared for the attempt to overturn the election. The committee referred Trump to the Justice Department for criminal prosecution, and the subsequent indictment by Special Counsel Jack Smith laid out a detailed case for Trump's criminal liability for the events of January 6.

Biden's approach to the COVID-19 pandemic represented a stark contrast to Trump's. Biden followed the guidance of public health experts, promoted vaccines, implemented mask requirements in federal settings, and worked to ensure an equitable distribution of vaccines. The vaccination campaign that Biden inherited from Trump was actually well-organized, and Biden successfully accelerated it to the point where the vast majority of American adults had been vaccinated by the summer of 2021. Biden also pushed for vaccination requirements for federal employees and for employees of large companies, requirements that were challenged in court and that the Supreme Court ultimately struck down for private employers while allowing them for healthcare workers. The COVID-19 death toll under Biden, though lower per capita than the worst of the pandemic under Trump, remained high, partly because vaccination rates among Republicans remained stubbornly low.

The economic challenges that Biden faced were real and difficult. The inflation that emerged in 2021 and 2022 was partly a consequence of the massive fiscal stimulus that Biden had supported, combined with supply chain disruptions caused by the pandemic and the impact of Russia's invasion of Ukraine on energy and food prices. Biden and his economic advisers initially characterized inflation as transitory, a claim that proved to be wrong as it persisted through 2022 and into 2023. The Federal Reserve responded by raising interest rates aggressively, which eventually brought inflation down but also slowed economic growth and made mortgages and loans more expensive for ordinary Americans. The lingering effects of high prices on the cost of living remained a major political liability for Biden and for Harris when she ran for president in 2024.

Kamala Harris's career trajectory was one of the most remarkable in American political history. Born to a Jamaican father and an Indian mother, Harris grew up in Oakland, California, and earned her law degree from the University of California Hastings College of the Law. She worked as a prosecutor in the Alameda County District Attorney's office before being elected District Attorney of San Francisco in 2003. She served two terms as San Francisco DA before being elected attorney general of California in 2010, becoming the first woman, first Black American, and first South Asian American to hold that office. She served two terms as attorney general before being elected to the Senate in 2016, replacing the retiring Democratic senator Barbara Boxer. Her Senate career, though brief, established her as a formidable questioner in Senate hearings and a strong progressive voice.

Harris's 2019 presidential campaign was widely anticipated as a major force, given her charisma, her debating skills, and her historic identity. But the campaign struggled from the start with strategic confusion about what lane Harris was running in, whether as a progressive or a moderate. She was effective in the debates, particularly her confrontation with Biden over busing and school desegregation in the first debate, but she struggled to convert debate momentum into support in the polls. She dropped out in December 2019, before the first primary votes were cast, citing a lack of money. Her decision to endorse Biden in March 2020 after he had secured the nomination put her in position to be selected as his running mate.

As vice president, Harris was given a difficult portfolio that included addressing the root causes of migration from Central America, protecting voting rights, and serving as a liaison to the Senate. Her performance on these assignments was criticized by both the press and by Democrats who felt she was underperforming. The immigration assignment was particularly fraught, as the root causes of migration were deep-seated and not easily addressed through diplomacy or aid programs. Her famous comment to potential migrants during a trip to Guatemala, do not come, was effective as a policy message but was criticized by progressives as too harsh. The voting rights legislation she championed died in the Senate when Manchin and Sinema refused to reform the filibuster to allow it to pass.

When Biden announced his withdrawal from the 2024 race in July 2024, Harris was the obvious choice to succeed him as the Democratic nominee. Her status as vice president, her fundraising network, and the party's commitment to diversity and inclusion all pointed in her direction. Within 24 hours of Biden's endorsement, Harris had secured the commitments of enough delegates to win the nomination, and the process of formally nominating her at the Democratic National Convention in Chicago was essentially a coronation. The convention was a high-energy affair that generated enormous enthusiasm among Democrats who had been despondent about Biden's prospects, and Harris left Chicago with a significant polling boost.

The debate between Harris and Trump in September 2024 was one of the most watched in American history. Harris prepared extensively and was widely judged to have won on points, landing effective attacks on Trump's character and record and defending her own positions with clarity and specificity. Trump was characteristically aggressive and dishonest, making multiple false claims and attacking Harris in personal and sometimes ugly ways. But the debate did not fundamentally change the dynamics of the race, with Trump maintaining a slight lead in the battleground states that mattered. The election came down to a few percentage points in Pennsylvania, Michigan, Wisconsin, Georgia, Arizona, and Nevada, and Trump won all of them.

Trump's second term began in January 2025 with a sweeping program of executive action that sought to transform the federal government in fundamental ways. Trump used executive orders to implement immigration restrictions, dismantle diversity and inclusion programs in the federal government, withdraw from international agreements, and begin the process of dismantling regulatory frameworks that had been built up over decades. The speed and scale of the executive actions reflected the lessons Trump and his allies had learned from the first term, when they had sometimes been slow to implement their agenda and had faced resistance from career officials who were committed to existing policies and procedures. In the second term, Trump moved quickly and decisively, replacing career officials with loyalists and using every available legal tool to advance his agenda.

The response to Trump's second term from Democrats and from civil society was fierce. Lawsuits challenged many of his executive actions, and some were blocked by courts. Protests were organized in cities across the country. Democratic governors announced their intention to resist federal policies they considered unconstitutional or harmful. But the structural realities of American politics meant that Democrats had limited tools to check a president who had unified government behind him and who was willing to push constitutional boundaries in ways that would require years of litigation to resolve. The fundamental question of whether American democratic institutions were strong enough to survive Trump's second term without permanent damage was one that Americans and the world would watch anxiously in the years ahead.

The story of Donald Trump is in many ways the story of a significant portion of the American electorate that felt left behind, disrespected, and ignored by the political establishment. Trump gave voice to their grievances and their resentment in a way that felt authentic, even when it was based on false claims and conspiracy theories. His supporters believed he was fighting for them against a corrupt system, and this belief proved extraordinarily durable in the face of everything that happened. The failure of the political establishment, the media, the business community, and the legal system to stop Trump or to persuade his supporters that he was unfit for office raised fundamental questions about the health of American democracy and the ability of democratic institutions to hold accountable a popular leader who defied their norms and their judgments.

The Democratic Party's challenge in the post-Biden era was to find a message and a messenger that could win back the working-class voters who had moved to Trump while holding on to the college-educated professionals and voters of color who formed the core of the modern Democratic coalition. This was a genuine political challenge with no easy answers, as the economic interests and cultural values of these different groups often pointed in different directions. The progressive wing of the party argued for a bold economic populism that addressed inequality and corporate power directly. The moderate wing argued for a pragmatic centrism that focused on kitchen-table economic issues and avoided the cultural fights that had alienated swing voters. The resolution of this debate would determine the direction of the Democratic Party for years to come.

The Republican Party faced its own long-term challenges, though these were less immediately pressing given its electoral successes. The demographic changes transforming America, with the proportion of white voters declining and the proportion of Latino, Asian American, and other non-white voters increasing, suggested that a coalition built primarily on white voters would face increasing headwinds over time. Republicans had made some gains with Latino voters under Trump, and these gains complicated the simple demographic story that Democrats had been telling about inevitable demographic advantage. But a party that had alienated African American voters almost entirely and that had done little to appeal to younger voters was taking a long-term risk that would eventually need to be addressed.

The health of American democracy at the close of the first quarter of the 21st century was a matter of genuine concern for those who studied democratic institutions and compared the United States to other advanced democracies and to its own history. The peaceful transfer of power, which had been a hallmark of American democracy since the founding, had been disrupted in 2021 in a way that was genuinely unprecedented. The norms that had governed presidential behavior, including honesty in public communications, respect for the rule of law, and deference to established institutions, had been shattered by Trump in ways that would be difficult to repair. The partisan polarization that made bipartisan governance nearly impossible had reached levels that many political scientists considered dangerous for democratic stability.

At the same time, there were grounds for cautious optimism about the resilience of American democracy. The courts, including courts appointed by Republicans, had consistently upheld the rule of law against Trump's most extreme demands. Election officials across the country had resisted enormous pressure to change results they had certified as accurate. The military had declined to be used as a political instrument even when Trump suggested they should be. The free press, though battered and diminished, continued to investigate and report on governmental misconduct. The ultimate transfer of power in January 2021, even after the January 6 attack, demonstrated that the institutions of American democracy were more resilient than many had feared. Whether they would continue to hold under the renewed pressure of Trump's second term was the central question of American political life.

The presidency of the United States has always been a reflection of the country that elects it. The presidents of the 21st century have reflected an America that is more diverse than ever before, more educated than ever before, and more interconnected with the rest of the world than ever before, but also more divided than it has been since the Civil War era, more uncertain about its economic future, and more anxious about its place in a rapidly changing world. The great achievement of American democracy has been to manage these divisions and anxieties through elections rather than through violence, to channel the deepest disagreements about the direction of the country into peaceful political competition rather than into armed conflict. This achievement has not been perfect, as January 6 demonstrated, but it has been real, and it represents a precious inheritance that Americans of all political persuasions have an interest in preserving.

The Republican Party and the Democratic Party are both imperfect vehicles for the aspirations of the American people, but they are the vehicles that American democracy has produced, and the contest between them will continue to shape the country's future. Republicans who believe in limited government, traditional values, and national sovereignty, and Democrats who believe in activist government, inclusive values, and international cooperation, will continue to compete for the presidency and for the ability to define what America stands for and where it is going. This competition, conducted through elections rather than through violence, is the essence of American democracy, and it is worth defending and preserving even at significant cost and effort.

The 21st century has been a challenging one for American democracy, marked by wars, economic crises, pandemics, and an unprecedented assault on democratic institutions by a sitting president. But it has also been a century of genuine democratic renewal, with the election of the first Black president, the nomination of the first woman and first woman of color for president, and the continuing expansion of political participation by previously marginalized groups. The story of American presidential politics in this era is a story of failure and achievement, of norm-breaking and institutional resilience, of division and occasional unity. It is a story that is still being written, with the outcome uncertain and the stakes as high as they have ever been in the history of the American republic.

The modern Republican Party as shaped by Donald Trump is a fundamentally different political organization than the one that existed under George W. Bush or even during the early Tea Party years. Trump did not just change the party's positions on trade, immigration, and foreign policy, though he did that. He changed its fundamental character, transforming it from a party with a coherent governing philosophy into a movement organized around loyalty to a single charismatic leader. Republican officials who had spent their careers building reputations for principled conservatism found themselves in the uncomfortable position of having to choose between their principles and their political survival, and most chose political survival. The few who chose otherwise, like Liz Cheney, the daughter of Dick Cheney who had served as House Republican Conference Chair before becoming one of Trump's most outspoken critics, found themselves ejected from the party's good graces.

Liz Cheney's transformation from loyal Republican to Trump's most prominent Republican critic was one of the most remarkable political stories of the era. She had voted with Trump 93 percent of the time during his presidency and had been a reliable conservative voice on matters of national security and fiscal policy. But her vote to impeach Trump after January 6 and her subsequent decision to serve on the House Select Committee investigating the attack made her a pariah in the Republican Party. She was stripped of her leadership position, censured by the Wyoming Republican Party, and defeated in her primary by a Trump-backed challenger. Her willingness to accept these consequences in order to speak the truth about what she had witnessed was widely admired by Democrats and by some Republicans, even as it ended her career as a mainstream Republican politician.

The phenomenon of Republican elected officials privately condemning Trump while publicly supporting him was one of the most disturbing aspects of the Trump era. Leaked texts, memoir accounts, and off-the-record conversations revealed that many Republican senators and House members believed Trump was unfit for office, had incited the January 6 attack, and was a danger to American democracy. But they supported him publicly anyway, calculating that opposing him would cost them their jobs without doing anything to stop him. This calculation was often correct in the short term, as Republicans who crossed Trump were indeed primary challenged and sometimes defeated. But it raised profound questions about the character and judgment of the elected officials who were supposed to serve as a check on presidential power.

The transformation of the Republican Party under Trump also transformed the party's relationship with truth and facts. Trump's willingness to make provably false statements, and the willingness of Republican officials and media figures to echo and amplify those false statements, represented a fundamental departure from the norms of democratic political discourse. The post-truth politics that scholars had warned about for years became the actual reality of Republican governance. Republican voters were told that the 2020 election had been stolen despite the absence of any credible evidence. They were told that COVID-19 vaccines were dangerous despite overwhelming scientific consensus that they were safe and effective. They were told that climate change was a hoax despite the near-universal consensus of climate scientists. The corrosive effect of this systematic disinformation on public trust in institutions, expertise, and democratic processes was one of the most damaging legacies of the Trump era.

The Democratic Party's response to Trump was complicated by its own internal divisions. The progressive wing of the party, represented by figures like Bernie Sanders, Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez, Ilhan Omar, and Rashida Tlaib, argued that Trump's rise was a consequence of the Democratic Party's failure to address economic inequality and corporate power, and that the solution was a bold progressive economic agenda that would win back the working-class voters who had defected to Trump. The moderate wing of the party, represented by figures like Biden, Pelosi, and the governors of states like Michigan, Wisconsin, and Pennsylvania, argued that progressivism was a liability in the battleground states and that the party needed to focus on economic kitchen-table issues and avoid the cultural fights that energized Republicans.

This internal debate was never fully resolved, and it contributed to the mixed Democratic performance in the elections of this era. Obama won by being liberal on some issues and moderate on others, assembling a coalition broad enough to win comfortable Electoral College majorities. Clinton lost partly by failing to generate sufficient enthusiasm among the progressive base while also failing to win back enough working-class whites in the Rust Belt. Biden won by being reassuringly moderate and by benefiting from the Trump fatigue that made even modest Democratic promises attractive to persuadable voters. Harris lost partly because she was unable to distinguish herself from the Biden administration's record in a way that addressed voters' economic anxieties.

The politics of abortion illustrates the complexity of the partisan divide on social issues in this era. Republicans had been opposing abortion rights since the 1970s, making the overturning of Roe v. Wade a central goal of the conservative movement for fifty years. When Trump's three appointments to the Supreme Court made that goal achievable, and when the Court actually overturned Roe in the Dobbs decision of 2022, many Republicans discovered that the political consequences were more severe than they had anticipated. American public opinion had long been divided on abortion, with majorities supporting some restrictions while also supporting the right to abortion at least in the early stages of pregnancy. When Republican-controlled states began implementing near-total bans, the political backlash was immediate and powerful, motivating Democratic voters and persuading many moderate Republicans to support Democratic candidates.

The question of gun control became another defining issue of the presidential era, with tragic mass shootings recurring with horrifying regularity and producing an annual cycle of Democratic calls for stricter gun laws and Republican opposition to any infringement on the Second Amendment. Sandy Hook, Parkland, Las Vegas, Orlando, Uvalde, Buffalo, and dozens of other massacres produced moments of national grief that faded without producing the kind of legislative action that most Americans said they supported. After the Uvalde school shooting in 2022, Biden signed the Bipartisan Safer Communities Act, the most significant gun legislation in nearly three decades, though it fell far short of what gun control advocates had sought. The fundamental divide between Republicans who saw gun ownership as a constitutional right that should not be infringed and Democrats who saw gun violence as a public health crisis that required legislative solutions was not resolved by this modest compromise.

The politics of healthcare remained one of the most fundamental divides between the parties throughout this period. Republicans spent years promising to repeal and replace the Affordable Care Act but could never agree on what the replacement should be. Their 2017 repeal effort collapsed in the Senate, partly because of the awareness that millions of Americans who had gained coverage under the ACA would lose it if it were repealed without a credible alternative. Democrats moved progressively further toward supporting a government-run universal healthcare system, with Medicare for All, the most ambitious version, becoming a litmus test for progressive candidates. Biden's more modest approach of building on the ACA and allowing people to buy into Medicare as a public option resonated more with moderate voters but disappointed progressives who wanted more fundamental change.

The question of America's role in the world was one of the most significant areas of disagreement between Republicans and Democrats in this period, and it was one where Trump's impact on the Republican Party was most dramatic. Republicans had been the more hawkishly internationalist party since the Cold War, supporting strong alliances, active military engagement, and American leadership in international institutions. Trump transformed the party into an America-first nationalist movement that was skeptical of alliances, hostile to international institutions, and focused on bilateral deals rather than multilateral frameworks. This transformation reflected genuine sentiment in the Republican base, which had grown tired of what it saw as America spending blood and treasure to defend other countries that did not appreciate it.

Democrats under Obama and Biden maintained a more traditional internationalist position, supporting NATO and other alliances, engaging with international institutions, and believing that American leadership in building and sustaining a rules-based international order served American interests. This position was increasingly difficult to maintain in the face of Republican attacks that characterized it as globalism that served the interests of elites at the expense of ordinary Americans. The debate about America's role in the world was not just about foreign policy but about identity, about what kind of country America wanted to be and who the country's government was primarily responsible to serve.

Trump's relationship with the news media was one of the defining features of his presidency. He attacked journalists and news organizations relentlessly, calling them the enemy of the people in terms that echoed authoritarian rulers throughout history. He gave exclusive access to friendly outlets like Fox News and One America News while treating mainstream journalists as adversaries. He held rallies at which attacking the press became a standard ritual, with crowds jeering and threatening journalists who were present to cover the events. The effect on public trust in the press was significant, with Republican voters increasingly distrustful of mainstream media and reliant on partisan outlets that confirmed their existing beliefs. This information ecosystem made it possible for Trump to maintain the support of his base even in the face of overwhelming evidence of his misconduct and dishonesty.

The story of American presidential elections in the 21st century is also a story of technological transformation. The 2000 election was still conducted in a relatively traditional media environment, with television advertising, newspaper endorsements, and ground-level organizing being the primary tools of presidential campaigns. By 2008, Obama had demonstrated the transformative power of the internet for political organizing and fundraising. By 2016, social media had become the dominant medium for political communication, and Trump had demonstrated that a candidate willing to use Twitter aggressively could drive news cycles and bypass traditional media gatekeepers. By 2024, the media ecosystem had fragmented even further, with podcasts, YouTube channels, and streaming platforms allowing candidates to reach targeted audiences in ways that were very different from the broadcast model that had dominated presidential politics for decades.

The role of money in presidential politics grew dramatically during this period, driven by the Supreme Court's Citizens United decision and its progeny. Super PACs funded by wealthy individuals and corporations could spend unlimited amounts on political advertising, and they did so in enormous quantities. The 2020 election, in which Biden raised and spent more than any previous presidential candidate in history, was the most expensive in American history. Trump also raised extraordinary amounts, primarily through small-dollar donations from his energized base. The flood of money into presidential politics did not obviously improve the quality of the campaigns or their ability to address the country's problems, but it did create a system in which very wealthy individuals had disproportionate influence over which candidates could compete for the presidency.

The decline of local journalism that accelerated during this period had significant consequences for presidential politics and for democratic governance more broadly. As newspapers closed and local TV news operations cut their staff, voters had less access to information about the activities of their local and state governments and less exposure to journalism that covered issues in their communities. This vacuum was partly filled by national media, social media, and partisan outlets, which tended to focus on national politics and on the most divisive and sensational stories. The result was that many Americans had strong opinions about national political figures while knowing relatively little about the officials and institutions that most directly affected their daily lives.

The institutions of American democracy outside the presidency also underwent significant stress during this period. Congress became increasingly dysfunctional, unable to pass major legislation on a bipartisan basis and prone to manufactured crises over routine matters like the debt ceiling and government funding. The Supreme Court became more explicitly partisan, with justices increasingly likely to rule in ways that benefited the party whose president had appointed them. The federal bureaucracy was buffeted by the competing demands of administrations with diametrically opposed philosophies, struggling to maintain institutional continuity and expertise in an environment of rapid political turnover. State and local government, though often more functional than the federal government, was also affected by the nationalization of politics that made even local elections into battles over national issues.

The presidency itself as an institution was tested severely during this period. The question of presidential immunity from criminal prosecution, long theoretical, became intensely practical as Trump faced criminal indictments on multiple charges after leaving office. The Supreme Court's decision in Trump v. United States, issued in 2024, significantly expanded the scope of presidential immunity from criminal prosecution for official acts, a decision that Democrats condemned as an invitation to presidential lawlessness and Republicans celebrated as a necessary protection of executive power. The decision raised fundamental questions about the accountability of presidents and the extent to which the rule of law applied equally to the most powerful officeholder in the country.

The American system of divided government, with its separation of powers and its system of checks and balances, was designed by the founders to prevent tyranny but also to make governance difficult. In a period of intense partisan polarization, the difficulty of governance has been compounded, as the checks and balances designed to require bipartisan consensus have instead produced gridlock. Each branch of government has been used by one party or the other as a weapon against the other party rather than as a partner in the difficult work of governing a complex and diverse country. The result has been a federal government that is increasingly unable to address long-term challenges, that oscillates between extremes as control passes between the parties, and that is regarded with deep distrust by a significant portion of the American public.

The American presidency as an institution has survived the extraordinary tests of the 21st century, but it has been changed by them. The norms that once governed presidential behavior have been shattered in ways that will be difficult to restore. The assumption that American presidents would respect the rule of law, accept the results of elections, and treat political opponents as adversaries rather than enemies has been challenged in fundamental ways. The restoration of those norms, or the establishment of new ones appropriate to the realities of contemporary American politics, is one of the central challenges facing the American political system in the years ahead. The presidency will continue to be the central institution of American democracy, for better or worse, and the choices made by those who occupy it and by those who vote for them will determine the future of the American experiment in self-governance.

The 2000 presidential contest between George W. Bush, the Republican governor of Texas, and Al Gore, the sitting Democratic vice president, ended in one of the most contested outcomes in the history of American elections. The margin in Florida was so tiny that automatic recount mechanisms kicked in, and the legal battle that followed consumed 36 days before the United States Supreme Court intervened. In a five to four ruling, the Court halted the recount, and Bush was declared the winner of Florida and therefore the president-elect. Gore had received approximately 540,000 more votes than Bush across the country, but the Electoral College system meant that the national popular vote was irrelevant. Democrats viewed the outcome as a profound injustice, a sense of grievance that would shape Democratic politics for years. The experience also convinced many Democrats of the importance of winning the Electoral College decisively rather than relying on popular vote margins, a lesson that influenced campaign strategy for decades.

Bush brought to the presidency a set of convictions rooted in his Christian faith, his Texas upbringing, and his family's long involvement in Republican politics. He believed in personal responsibility, in the power of free markets, and in America's responsibility to spread democracy and freedom around the world. These beliefs led him to support significant tax cuts that congressional Republicans passed in 2001 and 2003, cuts that reduced the income tax burden on Americans at all income levels but provided the largest absolute benefits to those with the highest incomes. Democrats argued the cuts were irresponsible, blowing up the budget surplus that Clinton had left behind and disproportionately benefiting the wealthy. Republicans argued they stimulated economic growth and returned money to the people who had earned it.

The September 11 attacks transformed Bush's presidency in ways that neither he nor anyone else could have predicted. The attacks created a moment of profound national unity that transcended partisan divisions, with Democrats and Republicans standing together on the steps of the Capitol to sing God Bless America in the days after the attacks. Congress authorized military force against the perpetrators of the attacks with only a single dissenting vote, that of Democratic Representative Barbara Lee of California, who warned against open-ended military commitments that could produce unintended consequences. Bush's approval ratings reached levels not seen since the earliest days of American public opinion polling, with nearly nine in ten Americans supporting him in the weeks after September 11.

The invasion of Afghanistan in October 2001 was broadly supported by the American public and by the international community. The Taliban government that had provided safe harbor to Osama bin Laden and al-Qaeda was universally condemned, and the United States assembled a broad coalition of allies to support the military operation. The initial phase of the war went well, with the Taliban government toppled quickly and al-Qaeda's training camps destroyed. But the failure to capture bin Laden at the Battle of Tora Bora in December 2001, when he is believed to have escaped across the Pakistani border, was a significant setback. The subsequent effort to stabilize Afghanistan and build a democratic government proved far more difficult than the initial military campaign, and the war dragged on for twenty years before America's final withdrawal in 2021.

The decision to invade Iraq in March 2003 was more controversial from the start. The Bush administration argued that Saddam Hussein had weapons of mass destruction, that he had links to terrorist groups including al-Qaeda, and that his removal would make the United States and the world safer. These arguments were contested at the time by many foreign policy experts and by several traditional American allies, including France, Germany, and Canada, which refused to participate in the coalition. The United Nations Security Council refused to authorize the invasion, and Secretary of State Colin Powell's presentation of intelligence about Iraqi weapons programs was later revealed to be based on flawed and in some cases fabricated evidence. Bush and Cheney proceeded with the invasion anyway, confident in their intelligence assessments and in the rightness of their cause.

The fall of Baghdad in April 2003 was swift, and Bush's declaration of the end of major combat operations under a Mission Accomplished banner on an aircraft carrier in May 2003 was premature and became one of the most mocked moments of his presidency. The occupation that followed was characterized by insufficient planning, inadequate troop levels, and a series of decisions that made the situation worse. The disbanding of the Iraqi army created hundreds of thousands of armed, unemployed men with grievances against the American occupation. The de-Baathification policy that barred former members of Saddam's party from serving in the new government eliminated many of the most competent administrators and security officials. The Abu Ghraib scandal revealed a culture of prisoner abuse that damaged America's reputation and undermined the moral justification for the war.

The 2004 election was the first national referendum on the Iraq War, and Bush won it narrowly. His message that changing horses in midstream would embolden the enemy and send the wrong signal to the world resonated with a significant portion of the electorate. Kerry's campaign was unable to present a clear alternative, partly because Kerry himself had voted for the war authorization and was vulnerable to the flip-flopper charge. The Swift Boat Veterans attacks on Kerry's military record were widely seen as dishonest, but they were effective at raising doubts about his character and fitness for command. Bush's victory, with 50.7 percent of the popular vote, gave him a narrow mandate for his second term, which he promised would be transformative.

The second term was anything but transformative in the ways Bush had hoped. His ambitious proposal to partially privatize Social Security was defeated when both Democrats and moderate Republicans rejected it as too risky. Hurricane Katrina exposed the inadequacy of the federal government's emergency management capabilities and the racial inequalities that left the most vulnerable Americans most exposed to disaster. The Iraq War continued to deteriorate, with sectarian violence between Sunnis and Shias escalating to near-civil war levels by 2006. The Democratic Party won control of both the House and Senate in the 2006 midterm elections, ending the Republican dominance of Congress that had existed since 1994. Bush's second term ended with the country mired in war and economic crisis, and his party deeply divided between those who wanted to maintain his legacy and those who wanted to distance themselves from it.

Barack Obama won the 2008 Democratic primary through a combination of grassroots organizing, superior fundraising through small-dollar internet donations, and an ability to inspire young and first-time voters. His campaign was a masterclass in modern political strategy, using data analytics and social networking to identify and mobilize supporters in ways that had never been done before. Obama's message of hope and change was vague enough to appeal to voters with very different ideas of what they wanted from a president, while being specific enough to establish him as a genuine progressive who intended to transform healthcare, energy policy, and America's role in the world. His victory over Hillary Clinton in the primary was a genuine upset against a candidate who had the endorsement of most of the Democratic establishment and had been seen as the inevitable nominee.

The general election against John McCain was made easier by the financial crisis that erupted in September 2008, just weeks before the election. McCain's response to the crisis was widely criticized as erratic and poorly handled, while Obama appeared calm and presidential. McCain's decision to suspend his campaign and return to Washington for the bailout vote, while Obama continued to campaign, was seen by many voters as a political stunt rather than a genuine act of leadership. Obama's message that the financial crisis was the result of eight years of Republican economic policies resonated with voters who had seen their retirement savings, home values, and job security devastated by the collapse. Obama won with 365 electoral votes, carrying several states that had not voted Democratic in decades.

Obama's presidency began with enormous expectations that were difficult to meet in the best of circumstances, and the circumstances were not the best. The financial crisis he inherited required immediate and massive intervention, leaving less political oxygen for the transformational domestic agenda he had promised. The stimulus bill that passed in February 2009 was significant but was also the subject of intense partisan controversy that established the pattern of Republican opposition that would characterize Obama's entire first term. Republicans called the stimulus wasteful and argued it would fail to revive the economy. Democrats argued it was necessary and saved the country from a second Great Depression. The economy did eventually recover, though more slowly than Obama had promised, and the question of how much credit the stimulus deserved remained contested.

The passage of the Affordable Care Act in March 2010 was the defining achievement of Obama's first term, fulfilling a promise that Democrats had been making for decades to provide universal or near-universal healthcare coverage. The law was a compromise that fell short of what many Democrats had originally sought, particularly the progressive dream of a single-payer Medicare for All system. The public option, which would have allowed Americans to buy into a government-run insurance program, was stripped from the bill to win the votes of moderate Democratic senators. But the law that emerged was nonetheless transformative, expanding Medicaid, creating insurance marketplaces, requiring coverage for pre-existing conditions, and extending coverage to tens of millions of Americans who had previously been uninsured.

The 2010 midterm elections were a referendum on Obama's first two years, and the results were devastating for Democrats. Republicans gained 63 House seats, the largest midterm swing in 72 years, and took control of the House. The Tea Party movement that had emerged in opposition to the stimulus and the ACA was responsible for many of these gains, bringing dozens of ideologically uncompromising conservatives to Congress who were committed to reducing government spending and opposing any compromise with Obama. The new Republican majority, led by Speaker John Boehner, clashed repeatedly with Obama over spending, taxes, and the debt ceiling, producing a series of manufactured crises and near-defaults that damaged the country's credit rating and economic confidence.

Obama's reelection in 2012 was a validation of his first term and a rejection of the Romney vision of dramatically smaller government. Romney was a capable and intelligent politician whose moderate Massachusetts governing record made him attractive to some general election voters but raised suspicions among the conservative primary electorate that had to nominate him. Romney's campaign was hurt by his comment at a private fundraiser that 47 percent of Americans were dependent on government and would never vote for him no matter what, a statement that was widely seen as revealing his disdain for ordinary Americans who received any form of government assistance. Obama won reelection with 332 electoral votes, running on a platform of rebuilding the middle class and requiring wealthy Americans and corporations to pay their fair share.

The 2016 election was a collision between the establishment politics that both parties had practiced for decades and the populist revolt that had been building since the financial crisis. Trump represented the Republican version of this revolt, channeling white working-class anger at immigration, free trade, and cultural change. Bernie Sanders represented the Democratic version, channeling progressive anger at economic inequality, corporate power, and a political system rigged in favor of the wealthy. Clinton represented the establishment of both parties, seen by many voters as a product of the same political world that had produced the problems they were angry about. Trump's populism proved more powerful in the Electoral College than Clinton's establishment centrism, and he won the presidency in a result that shocked the world.

Trump's first hundred days in office established the pattern of chaos, controversy, and partisan combat that would define his entire presidency. He issued executive orders on immigration that were blocked by federal courts. He fired FBI Director James Comey in a move that he later admitted was related to the Russia investigation. He failed to repeal the ACA despite promising to do so on his first day. He withdrew from the Trans-Pacific Partnership, the Paris Climate Agreement, and other international commitments. He made personnel decisions that puzzled allies and delighted adversaries. He communicated primarily through Twitter, which his White House declared official presidential statements but which often contradicted the statements of his own officials. The chaos was disorienting to traditional Washington but was seen by Trump's supporters as evidence that he was disrupting the comfortable arrangements of the political establishment.

The Mueller investigation lasted 22 months and produced a 448-page report that documented in detail the extent of Russian interference in the 2016 election and the Trump campaign's extensive contacts with Russian operatives. The report found that the Russian government had interfered in the election in a sweeping and systematic fashion, through a social media influence campaign and through the hacking and release of emails from the Democratic National Committee and Clinton's campaign chairman. The report also documented numerous instances of potential obstruction of justice by Trump, including his attempts to fire Mueller and to pressure officials to limit the investigation. Mueller declined to reach a conclusion on the obstruction question, citing Justice Department policy against indicting a sitting president, and left the decision to Congress.

Democrats argued that the Mueller report was a roadmap for impeachment and demanded action. Trump declared the report a total and complete exoneration, a characterization that was not supported by its actual contents. Republicans rallied around Trump and attacked the investigation as a partisan coup attempt. The first impeachment in December 2019, focused on the Ukraine affair rather than the Mueller findings, played out along exactly the same partisan lines. Trump was impeached by the Democratic House and acquitted by the Republican Senate. The second impeachment in January 2021, focused on incitement of insurrection, produced the same result, though with somewhat more Republican defections. Trump emerged from both impeachments with his hold on the Republican base undiminished.

Biden's presidency was defined by the dual challenges of the COVID-19 pandemic and the need to restore the institutions and norms that Trump had damaged. On COVID-19, Biden made significant progress in vaccine distribution, mask mandates in federal settings, and international coordination on pandemic response. He signed the American Rescue Plan, providing direct relief to Americans who were struggling with the economic consequences of the pandemic. He rebuilt relationships with European allies that had been strained by Trump's attacks on NATO and free trade. He rejoined the Paris Climate Agreement and the World Health Organization. He nominated the first Black woman to the Supreme Court, Ketanji Brown Jackson, who was confirmed with the support of three Republican senators.

Biden's foreign policy achievements included the management of Russia's invasion of Ukraine, which began in February 2022 and which Biden handled with considerable skill, rallying an unprecedented coalition of Western allies to impose severe economic sanctions on Russia and provide Ukraine with the military assistance it needed to resist the invasion. Biden also managed a series of difficult relationships in the Indo-Pacific, balancing the need to compete with China economically and militarily while avoiding direct conflict. His administration completed a chip technology agreement with the Netherlands and Japan to limit China's access to advanced semiconductor manufacturing equipment, a significant step in the effort to maintain American technological leadership.

The debate over Biden's legacy will continue for decades, but it is clear that his presidency produced significant achievements in domestic policy, foreign policy, and democratic governance that were underappreciated at the time. The Inflation Reduction Act's climate investments are among the largest in American history. The infrastructure law is reshaping American transportation, broadband access, and clean water systems in ways that will be visible for generations. The CHIPS and Science Act is rebuilding American semiconductor manufacturing capacity. Biden's management of the Ukraine crisis was a genuine diplomatic achievement that prevented what could have been a catastrophic Russian victory. These achievements were overshadowed by the high inflation that plagued his presidency and by the questions about his age and cognitive fitness that dominated his final year in office.

Kamala Harris will be remembered as a historic figure regardless of the outcome of the 2024 election. She was the first woman, first Black American, and first person of South Asian descent to serve as vice president. She was the first woman of color to be nominated for president by a major party. Her life story, from the daughter of immigrants who became a prosecutor, attorney general, senator, vice president, and presidential nominee, is a genuinely remarkable American story. Her political style, combining prosecutorial precision with a warmer personal approach than her 2019 campaign had shown, was well-suited to the moment when she became the nominee. Her defeat in 2024 reflected the structural difficulties that any Democratic candidate would have faced in that environment, not a repudiation of her personally or of the historic nature of her candidacy.

Donald Trump's place in American history is unique and will be debated for as long as the republic exists. He was the first president to be impeached twice. He was the first president to refuse to accept the results of a presidential election. He was the first president to face criminal indictments after leaving office. He was the first convicted felon to be elected president. He was the first president in 132 years to win non-consecutive terms. These firsts define a presidency that was unprecedented in its norm-breaking and in its impact on American democracy. Whether the ultimate verdict of history will be that he was a transformative leader who gave voice to a neglected portion of the American electorate or a dangerous authoritarian who damaged democratic institutions is a question that Americans will debate for generations.

The story of the American presidency from 2000 to 2025 is ultimately a story about what America decides it wants to be. Every election in this period was a choice between competing visions of the country's future, and the choices Americans made produced the presidents they got and the consequences that followed. The wars, the economic crises, the pandemic, the January 6 attack, and the ongoing struggle over democracy and its institutions were all the products of choices made by millions of Americans in voting booths across the country. The future of the American presidency and of American democracy will similarly be determined by the choices that Americans make in the elections to come. Understanding what happened in the first quarter of the 21st century, why it happened, and what it means is essential preparation for making those choices wisely.

The Republican Party's coalition under Trump consisted primarily of white voters without college degrees, evangelical Christians, rural and small-town Americans, and a growing share of working-class voters of various races who felt economically marginalized and culturally disrespected. These voters saw in Trump a champion who spoke their language, shared their grievances, and was willing to fight for them against a political establishment that had promised them things for decades and never delivered. Trump understood intuitively that these voters wanted results, not eloquence, and that they valued his willingness to be unconventional and confrontational over the polished competence of traditional politicians who had failed them. The loyalty he generated from these voters was unlike anything seen in modern American politics and proved durable in the face of every scandal, controversy, and legal challenge.

The Democratic Party's coalition under Obama and Biden consisted primarily of African Americans, Latinos, Asian Americans, college-educated whites particularly women, young voters, and urban professionals. This coalition had the demographic advantages of representing the growing sectors of the American population, but it had the geographic disadvantage of being concentrated in cities and coastal states that were already safely Democratic. The challenge of winning the Electoral College while losing rural and small-town America required Democrats to win the battleground states of the Midwest and Sun Belt by substantial margins, a challenge that proved increasingly difficult as working-class voters of all races drifted toward Trump. The tension between building a multiracial working-class coalition and maintaining the support of college-educated professionals who were motivated partly by cultural issues was one of the central strategic dilemmas of the Democratic Party throughout this period.

The fundamental conflict between the Republican and Democratic visions of America that has defined the presidential politics of the 21st century shows no signs of resolution in the near future. The structural forces that produced this conflict, including economic inequality, demographic change, cultural transformation, and technological disruption, are continuing and in some cases accelerating. The political institutions designed to manage conflict and produce consensus have been weakened by decades of partisan warfare. The shared civic culture that once provided common ground for political compromise has been eroded by media fragmentation and the polarization of public life. The American presidency, as the central institution of American democracy, will continue to be the primary arena in which these conflicts are contested, and the choices made by future presidents and future voters will determine whether the American experiment in self-governance emerges from this difficult period stronger or weaker.

The economic record of the 21st century presidents provides an important context for understanding their political fortunes. Bush inherited a surplus from Clinton and turned it into a deficit through tax cuts and war spending. Obama inherited the worst financial crisis in 80 years and presided over a slow but steady recovery that produced 75 consecutive months of private sector job growth by the end of his presidency. Trump inherited a strong economy and saw it strengthen further through 2019, with low unemployment and rising wages, before the COVID-19 pandemic destroyed millions of jobs in a matter of weeks. Biden inherited a recovering pandemic economy and presided over strong job creation but also the highest inflation in 40 years, which proved to be the most damaging issue of his presidency. Trump returned to inherit an economy that had largely recovered from the inflation of the Biden years, though the lasting effect of high prices on American living standards remained a source of discontent.

The military record of this period is one of the most consequential legacies of 21st century American presidential politics. Bush launched two wars that cost the country trillions of dollars and thousands of American lives. Obama reduced American military involvement in Iraq and Afghanistan but found himself drawn into new conflicts in Libya, Syria, and elsewhere. Trump threatened military action repeatedly but actually used force less than any recent president, though he also withdrew from several important agreements that had constrained adversaries. Biden ended the war in Afghanistan after 20 years, though the chaotic withdrawal damaged his presidency, and managed the Ukraine crisis primarily through economic sanctions and military assistance rather than direct American military involvement. The overall military record of this period reflects a country that is more cautious about direct military engagement than it was in the immediate post-September 11 years, having absorbed the painful lessons of Iraq and Afghanistan.

The judicial legacy of the 21st century presidents may be the most durable of all their legacies. Bush appointed two conservative justices. Obama appointed two moderately liberal justices. Trump appointed three strongly conservative justices, giving conservatives a six to three majority on the Supreme Court for the first time in decades. Biden appointed one liberal justice to replace the retiring Stephen Breyer. The six to three conservative majority has already transformed American law in significant ways, overturning Roe v. Wade, expanding gun rights, limiting regulatory power, and rolling back voting rights protections. The consequences of this judicial transformation will continue to unfold for decades, regardless of who occupies the White House or which party controls Congress. The battles over Supreme Court nominations have become the most consequential political contests of the era, with each nomination producing intense partisan combat that reflects the stakes involved.

The legacy of this period for American democracy is mixed, containing both causes for concern and causes for cautious optimism. On the concerning side, the assault on democratic norms and institutions under Trump, the January 6 attack on the Capitol, the systematic spread of disinformation about elections, and the increasingly partisan use of the legal system for political purposes all represent genuine threats to the foundations of democratic self-governance. On the optimistic side, American democratic institutions proved more resilient than many had feared, with courts, election officials, military leaders, and ultimately the voters themselves refusing to allow the peaceful transfer of power to be permanently disrupted. The participation of record numbers of Americans in elections, the activation of civil society in defense of democratic values, and the continued functioning of a free press despite persistent attacks all suggest that the foundations of American democracy, though stressed, remain intact.

The story of American presidential politics in the first quarter of the 21st century is not finished being written, and its ultimate meaning will be determined by what comes next. The choices made by American voters in elections still to come, the actions taken by presidents still to be elected, and the behavior of political institutions still under stress will determine whether this period is seen by future historians as a dangerous but ultimately manageable crisis that American democracy survived, or as the beginning of a more fundamental transformation that changed the character of American self-governance in lasting ways. The American presidency, as the central institution of the world's oldest continuous democracy, will be at the center of whatever comes next, and understanding its history in these extraordinary years is essential preparation for the challenges ahead.

When George W. Bush became the 43rd president of the United States, having defeated Democratic Vice President Al Gore in a bitterly contested election decided by the Supreme Court, he entered an office that seemed poised for a relatively uneventful period of domestic policymaking. The country was at peace, the economy was strong, and the budget was in surplus following the Clinton years. Bush had campaigned on education reform, Social Security reform, and a foreign policy of humility that would avoid the kind of nation-building interventions that had characterized the Clinton years. None of this came to pass, because the September 11, 2001 terrorist attacks transformed his presidency and with it the trajectory of American history for the next two decades.

Al Gore's concession in December 2000, following the Supreme Court's decision to halt the Florida recount, was one of the most dignified moments in modern American political history. Gore had won more votes from Americans across the country than Bush had, and he privately and publicly believed that if all the votes in Florida had been counted, he would have won that state too. But he chose to put the country's stability above his personal ambition, calling Bush to congratulate him and delivering a gracious concession speech in which he urged Americans to accept the outcome and move forward. The contrast between Gore's behavior in 2000 and Trump's behavior in 2020, when Trump refused to concede and instead launched a months-long campaign to overturn the results, was one of the starkest illustrations of how dramatically presidential norms had changed over two decades.

Bush's relationship with his father, former President George H. W. Bush, added a unique dimension to his presidency. He was only the second son of a president to himself become president, the first being John Quincy Adams two centuries earlier. The elder Bush had served as president from 1989 to 1993, losing his reelection bid to Clinton. Many observers noted that the younger Bush seemed determined to succeed where his father had not, including in finishing the job of removing Saddam Hussein from power in Iraq, something the elder Bush had chosen not to do when he had the chance after the first Gulf War in 1991. Dick Cheney and Donald Rumsfeld, both key figures in the younger Bush's national security team, had also served in senior positions under the elder Bush and other Republican presidents, bringing decades of Washington experience to an administration that the new president initially described as bringing a fresh perspective.

The war in Iraq, which began in March 2003 and continued for years after Bush left office, was the defining failure of his presidency. The decision to invade was based on the false premise that Saddam Hussein possessed weapons of mass destruction and the disputed claim that he had links to al-Qaeda and other terrorist organizations. The intelligence on which these claims rested was later shown to be deeply flawed, in some cases fabricated by informants seeking to manipulate American policy. The occupation that followed the relatively easy military victory was characterized by insufficient planning, inadequate troop levels, and decisions that destabilized the country and created the conditions for the insurgency and civil war that followed. The Iraq War cost over 4,400 American lives, hundreds of thousands of Iraqi lives, and over a trillion dollars, and left the Middle East more unstable than before.

Obama's election in November 2008 was celebrated around the world as a historic moment in the long struggle for racial equality in America. The grandson of a Kenyan goat herder became the most powerful person on earth, elected by a country that had enslaved Black people for 246 years and denied them full civil rights for a century beyond that. Obama's victory was made possible by the extraordinary organizing work of his campaign, which registered millions of new voters, mobilized young people who had never participated in politics, and built the most sophisticated ground operation in American political history. His election margin was comfortable but not overwhelming, winning 52.9 percent of the popular vote, but he carried states like North Carolina, Indiana, and Virginia that had not voted Democratic in decades, suggesting a realignment of American politics that many hoped would be durable.

The Affordable Care Act that Obama signed in March 2010 was the product of more than a year of contentious legislative battles, dozens of compromises, and a final dramatic passage that required every vote the Democrats had in both chambers. The law had been dramatically scaled back from its original ambitions, losing the public option that progressives had seen as the foundation of a path to universal coverage. But it still represented the most significant expansion of healthcare access since Medicare and Medicaid were enacted in 1965. The law's survival of Republican repeal efforts, court challenges, and years of political attack was testimony to the durability of social programs once they have been implemented and millions of people have come to rely on them. By the time Trump took office in 2017 and Republicans tried to repeal it, over 20 million Americans who had not previously had insurance were covered under the ACA, and the prospect of taking that coverage away proved deeply unpopular.

Obama's two terms were bookended by moments that revealed both the possibilities and the limitations of his presidency. His 2008 victory demonstrated that the impossible could happen in American politics, that an African American with an exotic name and a brief political resume could be elected president of the United States. His 2016 successor's victory demonstrated that the backlash against that possibility was equally powerful, that Trump's explicit appeals to racial resentment and cultural anxiety could mobilize enough voters to hand the Electoral College to a candidate who had never held public office and who was manifestly unqualified for the position by any traditional measure. The arc from Obama's election to Trump's election was not the arc of justice that Martin Luther King had famously described bending toward, but rather a reminder that progress is not linear and that every advance can produce a counterreaction.

Trump's promise to make America great again spoke to voters who felt that their country had lost something important, that the combination of economic globalization, cultural liberalism, and demographic change had transformed America in ways they did not want and had not consented to. These voters were not wrong that things had changed. The manufacturing towns of the Midwest and South had lost millions of jobs to overseas competition. The communities that had once organized around stable industrial employment were struggling with poverty, opioid addiction, and a loss of purpose. The cultural values that had organized life in these communities, including traditional religion, traditional gender roles, and an implicit assumption of white cultural dominance, were being challenged from every direction. Trump gave these voters someone to blame, someone to fight against, and a promise that their losses could be reversed.

Democrats struggled throughout this period to understand and respond to the working-class voters who had left their coalition. The party of Franklin Roosevelt and Harry Truman, which had built its majority on the votes of industrial workers, had gradually lost these voters as the party's cultural focus shifted from economic class to identity and as the party's economic policies became associated more with Wall Street and Silicon Valley than with the factory floor. Obama had made some progress in reconnecting with these voters through his economic message in 2008, but his presidency was seen by many working-class whites as having prioritized the interests of African Americans, immigrants, and college-educated professionals over their own. Trump's explicit appeal to these voters, with his attacks on free trade, his promises to bring manufacturing back, and his willingness to voice their cultural grievances without apology, proved irresistible to enough of them to swing the key Rust Belt states.

Biden's 2020 victory was built on a coalition similar to Obama's but with some important differences. Biden ran much better among college-educated whites, particularly women, than Obama had, a reflection of suburban voters' rejection of Trump's rhetoric and behavior. He ran somewhat worse than Obama among Black voters and Latino voters, a reflection of the gains Trump had made with these communities through his economic message and his willingness to engage directly with voters of color in ways that surprised many observers. Biden's message was less about transformational change and more about competence and decency, a simple promise to restore normalcy after four years of chaos and controversy. This message was sufficient to win the election but was not a mandate for the ambitious progressive agenda that many in his party wanted him to pursue.

The events of January 6, 2021 will be studied by historians and political scientists for generations. The assault on the Capitol building by thousands of Trump supporters who had been told by their president that the election had been stolen was unprecedented in modern American history. The attackers came from all over the country and from all walks of life, united by their devotion to Trump and their belief that extraordinary measures were necessary to prevent a fraudulent transfer of power. Many of them had been radicalized by months of disinformation spread through social media and right-wing media outlets. The attack was defeated not by the preparedness of law enforcement, which was caught badly flat-footed, but by the courage of individual officers and the ultimate refusal of Vice President Pence and other Republican officials to go along with Trump's demands.

Biden's first two years produced a legislative record that surprised many observers who had expected his narrow congressional majorities and his moderate reputation to produce modest results. The American Rescue Plan, the Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act, the CHIPS and Science Act, and the Inflation Reduction Act together represented the most significant domestic investment since the Great Society programs of the 1960s. Biden achieved these results through a combination of negotiation, pressure, and occasional willingness to accept half a loaf when the full loaf was not achievable. His success in passing bipartisan legislation, particularly the infrastructure bill, demonstrated that some degree of bipartisan cooperation was still possible even in the deeply polarized environment of the 2020s.

The Russia-Ukraine war that began with Putin's full-scale invasion in February 2022 was the most significant test of Biden's foreign policy and of the Western alliance that he had worked to rebuild after Trump's attacks on NATO. Biden's response, which included the most comprehensive package of economic sanctions ever imposed on a major power and billions of dollars in military assistance to Ukraine, was generally seen as effective in preventing Russia from achieving a quick victory. The war dragged on, however, and as American support became entangled in domestic political debates, the sustainability of the Western commitment to Ukraine became uncertain. Biden's management of the alliance was a genuine achievement, but the ultimate outcome of the war would not be determined before he left office.

Trump's return to power in January 2025 opened a new chapter in the ongoing struggle over the direction of American democracy and American foreign policy. His second administration moved quickly to implement an agenda that was even more radical than the first, reflecting the lessons learned from four years of experience and the determination not to be slowed by the internal resistance that had frustrated some initiatives in the first term. The long-term consequences of this second Trump presidency for American institutions, for the international order, and for the partisan balance of American politics remained to be seen, but the stakes could not have been higher or the moment more consequential for the future of American self-governance.

The presidency of George W. Bush must be understood in the context of the September 11 attacks, which transformed both the man and his office in ways that would define his legacy for all time. Before the attacks, Bush had been a conventional Republican president pursuing a conventional Republican agenda of tax cuts and education reform. After the attacks, he became a war president, defined by his determination to prevent future attacks and to defeat the terrorist organizations that had struck the American homeland. His approval ratings soared to nearly ninety percent in the weeks after September 11, reflecting a genuine national unity that transcended partisan divisions. That unity did not last, eroded by the Iraq War, Hurricane Katrina, and the financial crisis that brought his presidency to its troubled end.

The intelligence failure that led to the invasion of Iraq was one of the most consequential in American history. The intelligence community had convinced itself and its political masters that Saddam Hussein possessed weapons of mass destruction, based on a combination of wishful thinking, pressure from political leadership, and the testimony of unreliable informants. When the invasion revealed no weapons programs of any significance, the credibility of American intelligence and American leadership was severely damaged. The aftermath of the Iraq invasion created a new generation of terrorists, destabilized the region, and consumed American military and financial resources that could have been deployed elsewhere. The decision to invade Iraq will be debated by historians for as long as there are historians studying American foreign policy.

Obama's election was a watershed moment not just for African Americans but for the country as a whole. It demonstrated that the American ideal of equal opportunity could apply even to the highest office in the land, that a child born to an immigrant father and raised by his mother and grandparents in Hawaii and Indonesia could rise to lead the most powerful country in the world. Obama embodied the multicultural, cosmopolitan vision of America that Democrats had been promoting for decades, and his election seemed to vindicate that vision. His presidency revealed both how far America had come in the long struggle for racial equality and how far it still had to go, as the racial backlash that contributed to Trump's election demonstrated.

The Affordable Care Act represented the culmination of a decades-long Democratic dream to provide universal healthcare coverage to Americans. The law was not perfect, as its architects freely acknowledged, but it was a significant step toward the goal of ensuring that no American would go without medical care because they could not afford it. The Republican effort to destroy the law, which consumed enormous political energy for over a decade, reflected the depth of the ideological divide between the parties on the role of government in providing social insurance. Republicans believed that government mandates and subsidies distorted the healthcare market and violated individual freedom. Democrats believed that the market alone could never provide universal coverage and that government had a responsibility to ensure that all citizens had access to healthcare.

Trump's 2016 victory was the product of multiple converging forces, no single one of which was sufficient to explain it but all of which contributed. The long-term structural changes in the American economy that had hollowed out manufacturing communities. The cultural backlash against the demographic and values changes associated with the Obama years. The specific failures of Clinton's campaign, which failed to generate sufficient enthusiasm among the Democratic base and neglected the battleground states of the Midwest. The intervention of FBI Director James Comey eleven days before the election, when he announced the reopening of the investigation into Clinton's email server. Russian interference through social media manipulation and the hacking and release of damaging emails. Any one of these factors might have been manageable; together they produced a result that almost no one had predicted.

Trump's presidency forced American institutions to demonstrate whether they were strong enough to constrain a president who was determined to push the boundaries of executive power and who was willing to use deception and intimidation to advance his goals. The results were mixed. The courts blocked many of Trump's most extreme executive actions, particularly in the immigration area. The press continued to investigate and report on governmental misconduct despite being characterized as the enemy of the people. Senior officials sometimes refused to carry out unlawful orders. The military declined to be used as a political instrument. But Congress, which the founders had envisioned as the primary check on executive overreach, largely abdicated its responsibility, with Republicans choosing party loyalty over constitutional obligation. The Senate acquittal of Trump after both impeachments sent the message that a president of the same party as the Senate majority was effectively immune from accountability.

Biden's presidency was burdened from the start by the comparison to his predecessor and the expectations created by that comparison. After four years of chaos, dishonesty, and norm-breaking under Trump, many Americans expected Biden to restore the orderly, competent, and dignified governance that they associated with previous presidencies. Biden largely delivered on this expectation in terms of tone and process, but the expectations had been set so high that any shortcoming was magnified. His handling of the Afghanistan withdrawal, which ended a twenty-year war but produced chaotic scenes that shocked the country, damaged his presidency in ways that lingered. His response to the COVID-19 pandemic, while far more serious and science-based than Trump's, was complicated by vaccine hesitancy among Republicans that undermined public health goals. His management of inflation, while eventually successful, required years of painful price increases that devastated his approval ratings.

The Harris candidacy of 2024 was both a historic achievement and a political challenge. She became the nominee in extraordinary circumstances, with her predecessor withdrawing from the race just three and a half months before the election. She had to build a campaign organization from scratch, introduce herself to voters who had formed negative impressions of her during her time as vice president, and make the case that she would be different from Biden while still defending his record. She did all of this with considerable skill and energy, generating a genuine surge of enthusiasm that briefly made the race competitive. But the structural headwinds, including economic anxiety about inflation and immigration, proved too strong, and Trump won the key battleground states by margins that reflected the persistent advantage of his coalition in the Electoral College.

The story of the Republican and Democratic parties in the 21st century is ultimately a story about two different Americas, each with its own fears, hopes, and grievances, each convinced that the other side's vision would destroy the country. Republicans believed that the liberal cultural elite was attacking traditional American values, that open immigration was undermining American communities, that free trade had betrayed American workers, and that the administrative state was an unconstitutional usurpation of the powers that the founders had reserved to elected officials and to the people. Democrats believed that Trump and the Republican Party represented an existential threat to American democracy, that the conservative attack on voting rights was designed to entrench minority rule, and that the Republican economic agenda served the interests of the wealthy at the expense of everyone else.

Both of these narratives contained important elements of truth and important elements of exaggeration. American democracy did survive the Trump presidency, though it was tested in ways that left lasting damage. The Republican Party's economic policies did primarily benefit corporations and wealthy individuals, though they also produced economic growth that benefited many workers. The Democratic coalition did include cultural elites who sometimes seemed contemptuous of the values and concerns of working-class Americans, though the party's actual policy agenda was more focused on economic security than cultural issues. The polarized political environment made it difficult for citizens to engage seriously with the legitimate concerns of the other side, and the media ecosystem that rewarded outrage and conflict over nuance and complexity made the situation worse.

The future of American presidential politics will be shaped by forces that neither party fully controls. The continuing diversification of the American electorate will eventually force both parties to adapt their coalitions and their messages. The economic challenges posed by technological automation, climate change, and international competition will create new political pressures that may not map neatly onto the existing partisan divide. The generational turnover in political leadership, as the Baby Boomers who have dominated American politics for thirty years are gradually replaced by Generation X and Millennials, will bring new perspectives and new priorities to the competition for the presidency. How these changes interact with the deep partisan divisions and institutional weaknesses of the current period will determine whether American presidential politics becomes more functional and representative or continues on the path of polarization and dysfunction.

The American presidency as an institution has evolved significantly from what the founders intended. The founders envisioned a president who would be chosen by electors exercising independent judgment, who would be a figure above partisan politics, and who would exercise relatively limited powers within a system designed to prevent the concentration of authority. What emerged over two and a quarter centuries is a president who is chosen by a national popular vote mediated by the Electoral College, who is the leader of a political party and the primary target of partisan combat, and who exercises enormous powers through executive orders, regulations, and military authority that go far beyond what the founders contemplated. The modern presidency is both more powerful and more constrained than the founders envisioned, more powerful because of the demands of governing a complex modern state and conducting an active foreign policy, and more constrained by the checking mechanisms that have been developed through legislation, court decisions, and political norms.

The relationship between the presidential office and American political culture runs deeper than any individual president. The qualities that Americans look for in a president, the mixture of strength and humility, of vision and pragmatism, of conviction and open-mindedness, reflect the values that Americans claim to hold and aspire to embody. When a president fails to embody these qualities, as many have in different ways, it creates a sense of betrayal that can be more painful than mere policy disagreement. Trump's presidency was particularly disorienting for many Americans because his style and behavior so flagrantly violated the norms they associated with presidential dignity and character, while simultaneously appealing powerfully to a significant portion of the electorate that valued his authenticity and his willingness to fight for them regardless of decorum.

The role of the First Lady has also evolved during this period. Laura Bush was a traditional First Lady who focused on literacy and education, presenting a warm and dignified presence that was a counterbalance to her husband's sometimes combative style. Michelle Obama transformed the role, becoming one of the most popular figures in American politics through her Let's Move campaign against childhood obesity, her advocacy for military families, and her powerful speeches that connected personal narrative to broader themes of American aspiration. Melania Trump was a more withdrawn First Lady who maintained a low profile and sometimes seemed to distance herself from her husband's controversies. Jill Biden, a career educator who continued teaching community college during her husband's presidency, was a more engaged and politically active First Lady who played a significant role in the 2024 succession drama.

The vice presidents of this era also played significant roles that shaped the presidential legacy. Dick Cheney was the most powerful vice president in American history, exercising extraordinary influence over national security policy and the expansion of executive power. Joe Biden was a more traditional vice president but an important legislative liaison and trusted partner who played a crucial role in managing the economic crisis of Obama's first term. Mike Pence served as the loyal conservative reassurance for Republican voters who might otherwise have been uncertain about Trump, and his refusal on January 6 to go along with Trump's demand that he reject the certified electoral votes was one of the most consequential individual acts in the Trump era. Kamala Harris made history as the first woman, Black American, and South Asian American to serve as vice president, and her ultimate nomination as the Democratic presidential candidate represented the full realization of her historic role.

Every president in this era faced the question of what America's role in the world should be, and every president answered it differently. Bush believed in American exceptionalism and the responsibility to spread democracy by force if necessary. Obama believed in multilateral cooperation and the limits of American power to impose outcomes on complex societies. Trump believed that America had been taken advantage of by its allies and trading partners and needed to put its own interests first, making deals rather than maintaining commitments. Biden believed in rebuilding the alliances that Trump had damaged and reasserting American leadership in a world challenged by authoritarian resurgence. Each of these visions reflected genuine convictions and genuine constituencies, and the oscillation between them over four presidential terms left America's allies and adversaries uncertain about what America's long-term commitments were.

The economic legacies of these presidents are complex and intertwined with global forces that no president can fully control. Bush inherited prosperity and left economic disaster. Obama inherited disaster and produced recovery. Trump inherited recovery and produced further growth before the pandemic upended everything. Biden inherited the pandemic economy and produced strong job growth alongside troubling inflation. The question of which presidents deserve credit or blame for the economic conditions of their tenures is complicated by the fact that economic trends often take years to materialize and that global forces beyond any president's control play a larger role than any domestic policy. What is clear is that all four presidents grappled with the fundamental challenge of managing an economy that was producing enormous wealth for some Americans while leaving many others behind, and that none of them fully solved this challenge.

The contested Florida recount of 2000 became one of the most examined episodes in American electoral history, with partisan interpretations solidifying over the years in ways that mirrored the broader polarization of the country. Democrats continued to believe that Bush had stolen the election, pointing to the Supreme Court's five to four decision along partisan lines to halt the recount as evidence of judicial partisanship. Republicans argued that Bush had won fair and square, that the recount rules that Gore's lawyers were seeking would have been unfair and selective. Independent analyses of the uncounted Florida ballots reached different conclusions depending on which counting method was applied, with some suggesting Gore would have won under some standards and Bush under others. The uncertainty itself was part of the damage, creating a sense that the most powerful office in the world had been decided by factors that had nothing to do with the will of the voters.

Bush's faith-based approach to governance was reflected in many aspects of his presidency, from his personal style of leadership to his policy priorities. He was the first president to so explicitly incorporate his Christian faith into his decision-making, speaking frequently about consulting prayer before major decisions and describing himself as chosen by God for the moment. This faith was genuine and not merely political, reflecting a personal transformation that had occurred when he stopped drinking in his late thirties. But it also contributed to a certainty about his decisions that critics argued made him unwilling to acknowledge mistakes or change course even when circumstances required it. The Iraq War was the clearest example of this pattern, with Bush continuing to defend the decision to invade even as the evidence of its failure mounted.

The surge of American troops into Iraq in 2007 was Bush's last major strategic decision and one of his most consequential. Against the advice of many military commanders and despite the strong opposition of Democrats in Congress, who had won their majorities in 2006 largely by opposing the war, Bush sent an additional 30,000 troops to Iraq and changed the strategic focus from handing off to Iraqi security forces to directly protecting the civilian population. The surge, commanded by General David Petraeus, succeeded in reducing violence significantly and stabilizing enough of the country to allow a political process to begin. Republicans pointed to the surge as a vindication of Bush's determination and a demonstration that the war could be won. Democrats argued that the political reconciliation the surge was supposed to enable never fully happened and that the stability it produced was fragile.

Obama's presidency represented a fundamental change in the style and substance of American leadership, not just in the obvious symbolism of having a Black president but in the approach to governance and to America's role in the world. Obama was the most intellectually sophisticated president since Woodrow Wilson, a former constitutional law professor who thought carefully about the limits and purposes of presidential power. His deliberative style, which involved hearing from multiple advisors and considering multiple perspectives before making decisions, was widely praised by those who worked with him but was sometimes criticized as excessive caution or paralysis in a world that required decisive action. His willingness to acknowledge American mistakes and limitations was celebrated by allies who had felt lectured by Bush but was condemned by Republicans who saw it as an apology tour that weakened America's global standing.

The rise of social media transformed presidential politics in ways that no one fully anticipated when Twitter and Facebook launched in the mid-2000s. Obama's 2008 campaign was the first to use social media systematically and effectively, building a grassroots organizing network that allowed the campaign to identify, mobilize, and communicate with supporters in ways that had never been possible before. But it was Trump who fully understood and exploited social media's potential to bypass traditional media gatekeepers and communicate directly with supporters. Trump's Twitter feed was a constant source of news, controversy, and provocation that kept him in the center of the political conversation every day and made it virtually impossible for his opponents to control the narrative. The direct, unfiltered communication that social media enabled was simultaneously one of Trump's greatest political assets and one of the greatest threats to the norms of civil democratic discourse.

The role of race in American presidential politics became more explicit during the Obama and Trump years than at any time since the civil rights movement. Obama's election represented the triumph of the ideal of racial equality, the fulfillment of the promise that any child, regardless of the color of their skin, could aspire to the highest office in the land. But his presidency also provoked an explicit racial backlash that found expression in the birther conspiracy theory, in the Tea Party movement, and ultimately in Trump's campaign, which made immigration restriction, the building of a wall against Mexican immigrants, and attacks on Latino politicians and judges central elements. The explicit racial coding of Trump's political appeal was one of the most disturbing aspects of his rise, representing a willingness to use racial resentment as a political weapon that had no modern precedent at the presidential level.

The Democratic Party's struggle with its own complex history on racial issues was also part of this story. The party of the New Deal, which had included Southern segregationists alongside Northern liberals, had transformed into the party of civil rights under Johnson, losing the South in the process. Obama represented the full flowering of this transformation, a Black president who had won with the overwhelming support of African American voters and significant support from white liberals. But the party's relationship with working-class white voters, who had been part of the Roosevelt coalition but who had been drifting away for decades, was deeply damaged, and Trump's success in accelerating this drift demonstrated the limits of the multicultural coalition that Obama had assembled.

Biden's decision to withdraw from the 2024 race was one of the most consequential and most discussed decisions of his presidency. The decision was triggered by his disastrous debate performance against Trump in June 2024, which raised serious questions about his ability to campaign effectively for another four months and to govern for another four years. But the underlying concerns about his age and fitness had been building for years, and many Democrats had privately worried that he was not up to the demands of the race even before the debate. Biden's decision to step aside was praised by many as an act of selfless patriotism and condemned by others as a belated recognition of a problem that should have been addressed earlier. The compressed timeline that resulted gave Harris little time to build her campaign and meant that Democrats went into the fall campaign at a significant disadvantage in terms of organization and messaging.

The Republican Party's evolution under Trump was not just about policy positions but about the fundamental character of politics. Traditional Republican politics had been about ideas and principles, about limited government, free markets, and strong national defense. Trump transformed the party into a vehicle for personal loyalty and cultural warfare, in which the primary loyalty was to Trump himself rather than to any set of principles, and in which the primary conflict was against the liberal cultural elite rather than for any positive vision of governance. This transformation was welcomed by many Republican voters who had grown frustrated with a party establishment that talked about conservative principles but failed to deliver results, and who appreciated Trump's willingness to fight for them without apology. But it raised serious questions about the long-term health of a party whose primary organizing principle was loyalty to a single individual.

The Democratic Party's internal divisions in this period reflected the genuine complexity of its coalition and the genuine difficulty of the strategic and policy choices it faced. The progressive wing, represented by Sanders, Warren, Ocasio-Cortez, and others, argued for bold transformational change on healthcare, climate, and economic inequality, believing that timid incrementalism had failed to address the fundamental challenges facing working people. The moderate wing, represented by Biden, Pelosi, and the governors of competitive states, argued that bold progressivism was a liability in the battleground states and that the party needed to focus on pragmatic solutions to the economic anxieties of the median voter. Neither wing had a complete answer, as the mixed electoral results of the period demonstrated, and the tension between them continued to complicate the party's ability to present a unified message to voters.

The role of women in presidential politics changed dramatically during this period. In 2000, there was no woman on either major party presidential ticket. By 2008, Sarah Palin had become the first woman on the Republican presidential ticket, though her selection was widely seen as a political stunt rather than a serious acknowledgment of women's political advancement. Hillary Clinton in 2016 was the first woman to win a major party presidential nomination, and her loss to Trump was interpreted by many women as a painful reminder that gender equality at the highest levels of American politics had not yet been achieved. Kamala Harris as vice president and then as the 2024 Democratic presidential nominee represented continued progress, and her campaign generated an outpouring of enthusiasm from women who saw her candidacy as historic and meaningful regardless of the outcome.

The economic anxiety that drove American presidential politics in this period was rooted in real changes in the American economy that had been building for decades. The deindustrialization of the Midwest and South, accelerated by trade agreements and technological automation, had eliminated millions of manufacturing jobs that had provided middle-class incomes to workers without college degrees. The financial crisis of 2008 wiped out the retirement savings and home equity of millions of Americans and exposed the vulnerability of an economy built on financial speculation rather than productive investment. The recovery from the financial crisis, while real, was slow and unequal, with corporate profits and stock prices recovering quickly while wages stagnated and many communities continued to struggle. The inflation of the Biden years, though eventually brought under control, added to the sense that the economic system was not working for ordinary people even when the headline numbers looked good.

Both parties offered responses to this economic anxiety, but neither fully addressed its root causes. Republicans offered tax cuts, deregulation, and trade restrictions, arguing that these policies would stimulate economic growth and bring jobs back to American communities. Democrats offered healthcare expansion, worker protections, and investments in education and infrastructure, arguing that these policies would provide greater economic security and opportunity for working people. Neither approach fully addressed the fundamental structural changes in the economy, and both parties sometimes prioritized the interests of their major donors over the interests of the working people they claimed to champion. The persistence of economic anxiety across multiple presidencies, despite genuine policy differences between the parties, suggested that the challenges were deeper than either party's standard solutions could address.

The story of how the most powerful country in the world chose its leaders over the first quarter of the 21st century is a story of extraordinary events, consequential choices, and ongoing struggles over the meaning of democracy and the direction of the nation. The wars, crises, and controversies of this period will be studied by future generations seeking to understand how America navigated the transition from its post-Cold War predominance to a more complex and contested world. The presidency, as the institution through which these choices were made and these crises managed, will remain at the center of this story, both as a reflection of American society and as a shaper of American destiny.

Republican politics and Democratic politics in the 21st century have both been transformed by forces that were set in motion decades earlier but that reached their full expression in this period. The conservative movement that began with Barry Goldwater in 1964 and achieved its first great triumph with Reagan in 1980 evolved through multiple stages before arriving at the Trumpism of the 2010s and 2020s. The progressive movement that emerged from the New Deal and the Great Society and the civil rights revolution evolved through Clinton's Third Way centrism and Obama's multicultural liberalism before the Bernie Sanders insurgency pushed it leftward in ways that complicated the party's electoral strategies. These long-term trajectories intersected with specific events and specific personalities in ways that produced the particular outcomes of this era, but they also reflected deeper structural forces in American society that any future president will have to navigate.

The presidency as an institution and as a symbol has been through extraordinary stress in this period, and its future will depend on whether Americans can find ways to rebuild the trust in institutions that has been eroded by decades of polarization, dysfunction, and sometimes outright dishonesty in high office. The American founders designed a system that could survive bad leaders because the institutions would constrain them, but they could not have anticipated a situation in which a significant portion of the population rejected the legitimacy of the institutions themselves. Rebuilding that legitimacy, or finding new foundations for it, is one of the central challenges facing American democracy in the years ahead, and the presidency will inevitably be both a cause of and a potential solution to that challenge.

The first presidential election of the new millennium was a reminder that in American democracy, nothing can be taken for granted and every vote can matter in ways that no one anticipates. George W. Bush and Al Gore had campaigned for months across the country, spending hundreds of millions of dollars, giving thousands of speeches, and participating in nationally televised debates that millions of Americans watched. On election night, the networks initially projected Gore as the winner of Florida, which would have given him the presidency. Then they retracted that call and projected Bush as the winner. Then they retracted that call too, declaring the state too close to call. The final margin, after weeks of legal battles and a Supreme Court decision, was 537 votes out of nearly six million cast in Florida. The lesson that every vote matters, that elections can be decided by razor-thin margins, was driven home with a force that transformed American electoral politics for a generation.

Bush's first term policy priorities were overshadowed by September 11, but his domestic achievements were real. The No Child Left Behind Act, passed with bipartisan support including from Democrat Ted Kennedy, represented a genuine effort to improve educational outcomes for disadvantaged students through increased testing and accountability. The Medicare Modernization Act of 2003 added a prescription drug benefit to Medicare, the largest expansion of that program since its creation in 1965. These were genuine accomplishments that reflected Bush's compassionate conservatism philosophy, his belief that government could play a positive role in addressing social problems while maintaining Republican priorities of market mechanisms and limited federal control. But these achievements were obscured by the Iraq War and the Katrina disaster, which defined his presidency in the public mind.

Obama came to the presidency with a mandate for change and a vision of a more united, more equitable, and more internationally engaged America. He assembled a talented team of advisors and cabinet officials, drawing on Democratic foreign policy expertise, academic economics, and the institutional knowledge of Washington veterans. His initial years in office were consumed by the economic crisis, which required immediate and massive intervention to prevent the recession from becoming a depression. The Recovery Act, as Democrats called the stimulus, was imperfect and insufficient in the view of many economists, but it stopped the bleeding and put the country on the path to recovery. Obama's patience and his refusal to panic in the face of political pressure were qualities that served him well in managing the crisis.

The Republican Party's response to Obama was shaped partly by ideology and partly by the political calculation that unified opposition would pay electoral dividends. Mitch McConnell famously declared in 2010 that the single most important thing Republicans could accomplish was to make Obama a one-term president. This wasn't just cynical politics; it reflected a genuine Republican belief that Obama's agenda was moving the country in a fundamentally wrong direction and that the best thing for the country was to stop it. But it also represented a willingness to put partisan advantage above the national interest that was damaging to the institution of Congress and to the norms of bipartisan cooperation that had allowed the government to function for generations. The obstruction strategy had mixed results, as Obama won reelection in 2012, but it did succeed in limiting his second-term accomplishments.

The rise of Trump from reality television star to president of the United States was a story that could only have happened in the America of the 2010s, a country in which celebrity and entertainment had become more important than experience and expertise, in which social media had created new channels of political communication that bypassed traditional gatekeepers, and in which a significant portion of the electorate was so disgusted with conventional politics that they were willing to take a chance on an entirely unconventional candidate. Trump had been a public figure for decades, famous for his luxury real estate developments, his multiple business ventures and bankruptcies, and his long-running reality television program The Apprentice. He was a master of self-promotion and an instinctive communicator who understood how to generate attention and control narratives. These skills, which had served him well in the entertainment business, proved surprisingly transferable to presidential politics.

Trump's relationship with facts was unlike that of any previous major party presidential candidate or president. He made false statements with a frequency and brazenness that fact-checkers struggled to keep up with, eventually cataloguing thousands of misleading or false claims over the course of his presidency. He denied saying things that were on video. He invented statistics. He claimed credit for things that had happened under his predecessors. He made predictions about the future that were contradicted by the evidence available. And through it all, his supporters remained loyal, either believing him, or disbelieving the fact-checkers, or concluding that the dishonesty didn't matter because politicians had always lied and at least Trump was honest about being on their side. This immunity to the normal political consequences of dishonesty was one of the most remarkable features of Trump's political career.

Biden represented a return to a more traditional form of Democratic politics, one that emphasized empathy, experience, and decency rather than the transformational rhetoric of Obama or the democratic socialism of Sanders. Biden's long career in the Senate had made him a master of the institutional politics of Washington, of building coalitions across ideological lines and finding the compromises that could assemble 60 votes in the Senate or pass legislation through a narrowly divided House. His experience as Obama's vice president had given him an intimate knowledge of the executive branch and the challenges of governing. His personal story, marked by tragedy and resilience, had given him an authenticity and an ability to connect with ordinary Americans that more polished politicians sometimes lacked. These qualities made him an effective candidate in 2020 and a reasonably effective president in the first half of his term, though his age eventually became an insurmountable political liability.

Kamala Harris represented the future that many Democrats hoped for, a multiracial coalition led by a woman of color who could speak to the aspirations of a diverse America. Her prosecutorial background, her Senate career, and her vice presidential experience had prepared her well for the demands of a presidential campaign in terms of substance, but the compressed timeline of her 2024 campaign meant she was introducing herself to much of the country under conditions of maximum scrutiny and minimum preparation time. She ran an impressive campaign given the circumstances, generating genuine enthusiasm, holding her own in the debate against Trump, and presenting a coherent vision of an America that worked for everyone. But the structural disadvantages she faced, including the incumbency penalty for the party in power during a period of inflation and the geographic concentration of Democratic voters in non-battleground states, proved too much to overcome.

The story of American presidential politics in this era is inseparable from the story of economic change and economic anxiety. The financial crisis of 2008 destroyed the savings and security of millions of Americans and shattered confidence in the institutions that were supposed to be managing the economy responsibly. The recovery was real but slow and unequal, with the gains going disproportionately to those who already had assets while those who depended on wages struggled. The opioid epidemic that devastated many rural communities was partly a product of this economic despair, as communities that had once organized around stable employment found themselves without purpose or hope. Trump's promises to bring jobs back, to confront the trade deals that had sent manufacturing overseas, and to put America first in its economic arrangements spoke directly to these anxieties in a way that conventional Democratic and Republican politics had failed to do.

The impact of American presidential decisions on the rest of the world is a dimension of this history that should not be overlooked. The decision to invade Iraq affected millions of Iraqis, produced a refugee crisis that transformed the politics of Europe, and contributed to the rise of the Islamic State which terrorized populations across multiple countries. Obama's management of the Arab Spring, which included supporting the overthrow of longtime American ally Hosni Mubarak in Egypt and intervening militarily in Libya, contributed to instability that produced humanitarian catastrophes in Libya, Syria, and elsewhere. Trump's withdrawal from the Iran nuclear deal contributed to Iran's acceleration of its nuclear program. Biden's management of the Ukraine war affected the security and prosperity of millions of Europeans while testing the strength of the Western alliance. These global consequences of American presidential decisions are part of the full accounting of this era's legacy.

The succession of American presidents from Bush through Obama, Trump, and Biden represents a remarkable range of governing philosophies, personal styles, and historical circumstances. Bush was the conventional Republican pushed to extremity by the September 11 attacks and their aftermath. Obama was the transformational progressive constrained by economic crisis and partisan opposition. Trump was the populist nationalist who disrupted the norms of American governance in ways that both reflected and accelerated the country's polarization. Biden was the institutionalist who sought to restore normalcy but found that normalcy was harder to restore than he had hoped. Each of these presidents left a mark on the office and on the country that will endure for decades, and the interaction of their legacies will shape the debates of American politics for generations to come.

The Republicans and Democrats who contested the presidency in this period were not just competing for power; they were competing to define America. The Republican vision, as it evolved from Bush's compassionate conservatism through Tea Party constitutionalism to Trump's nationalist populism, argued for an America that was strong, traditional, and unapologetically self-interested. The Democratic vision, as it evolved from Clinton's Third Way centrism through Obama's multicultural liberalism to the Sanders-influenced progressivism of recent years, argued for an America that was inclusive, internationally engaged, and committed to using government to address inequality and injustice. These competing visions reflected genuine differences in values, interests, and interpretations of American history and destiny, and the elections that chose between them were not just political contests but arguments about what America is and what it should be.

The presidency will continue to be the central institution through which these arguments are made and these choices are implemented. The power of the modern presidency, built up over two centuries of constitutional interpretation and political practice, means that who occupies the office matters enormously for the direction of the country and the world. The elections that choose who occupies it are the most consequential democratic exercises in the world, and understanding what has driven their outcomes in the 21st century is essential to understanding both the promise and the peril of American democracy in the years ahead.
